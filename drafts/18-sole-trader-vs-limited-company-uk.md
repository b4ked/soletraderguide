# **Sole Trader vs Limited Company: Which Is Right for You? (2026)**

Choosing between sole trader and limited company is one of the most common questions for self-employed people in the UK — and one of the most misunderstood. This guide gives you a clear, honest comparison so you can make the right decision for your situation.

Short answer: most freelancers and sole traders are better off staying as sole traders until profits consistently exceed £40,000–£50,000. Here's why, and when that changes.

## **The key differences at a glance**

| | Sole Trader | Limited Company |
|---|---|---|
| Setup | Register with HMRC | Incorporate at Companies House |
| Tax on profits | Income tax + NI | Corporation tax (19–25%) |
| Personal liability | Unlimited | Limited to your investment |
| Admin | Self Assessment only | Annual accounts + confirmation statement + payroll |
| Privacy | No public filing | Accounts filed publicly |
| Credibility | Lower perceived status | Higher perceived status |
| MTD from April 2026 | Quarterly submissions required | Corporation tax rules (different system) |

## **Tax: How much will you actually pay?**

This is where most people start — and it's more nuanced than it appears.

**As a sole trader (2025-26 figures):**
- Personal allowance: £12,570 (tax-free)
- Basic rate: 20% on profits £12,571–£50,270
- Higher rate: 40% on profits £50,271–£125,140
- Class 4 NI: 6% on £12,570–£50,270, then 2% above

**As a limited company (2025-26 figures):**
- Corporation tax: 19% on profits up to £50,000, 25% above £250,000 (marginal relief between)
- You take a salary (usually set at the NI threshold ~£12,570/year) and the rest as dividends
- Dividend tax: 10.75% (basic), 35.75% (higher), 39.35% (additional rate)
- Dividend allowance: £500

**When the maths tips in favour of a limited company:**

At lower profit levels (under £35,000–£40,000), sole trader and limited company tax bills are very similar once you account for accountancy costs and the reduced dividend allowance. The traditional "£30k profit = go limited" advice no longer holds.

Most accountants now suggest the incorporation threshold is around £40,000–£50,000 in consistent profit — and even then, the net saving after increased accountancy fees (typically £800–£2,000/year for limited company accounts vs £300–£600 for sole trader) needs to be meaningful.

**Worked example at £50,000 profit:**
- Sole trader: approx £13,000–£14,000 in income tax + NI
- Limited company (optimal salary + dividends): approx £10,000–£11,000 in corporation tax + dividend tax
- Net saving: roughly £2,000–£3,000/year — meaningful, but narrowing

## **Liability: why it matters (and when it doesn't)**

**Sole trader:** you and your business are the same legal entity. If your business owes money or is sued, your personal assets (savings, car, home) are at risk.

**Limited company:** your liability is limited to the amount you've invested in the company. Personal assets are protected in most circumstances.

**When liability protection matters most:**
- You work in high-risk industries (construction, professional advice, healthcare)
- You have significant personal assets to protect
- You work on contracts where disputes and claims are realistic

**When it matters less:**
- You have professional indemnity insurance (which most freelancers should have regardless of structure)
- Your work is low-risk service delivery
- You have few personal assets to protect

## **Admin: what you're actually signing up for**

This is where sole traders often underestimate the difference.

**Sole trader admin per year:**
- One Self Assessment return
- Keep income and expense records
- MTD quarterly submissions from April 2026 (if earnings over £50k)

**Limited company admin per year:**
- Annual accounts (prepared and filed with Companies House)
- Corporation tax return (CT600)
- Confirmation statement (Companies House)
- Director's Self Assessment return
- Payroll / PAYE if paying yourself a salary
- Dividend vouchers and board minutes

A limited company typically needs an accountant. Expect to pay £800–£2,000/year more in accountancy fees than you'd pay as a sole trader. Factor this into your tax saving calculation.

## **When does incorporation make financial sense?**

The rule of thumb most UK accountants apply in 2026:

- **Under £35,000 profit:** stay as a sole trader. The tax saving doesn't justify the extra costs and admin.
- **£35,000–£50,000 profit:** start evaluating. Run the numbers with an accountant. The saving may be £1,000–£2,000/year after costs.
- **Over £50,000 profit:** incorporation usually makes sense. Tax savings become more meaningful, and liability protection becomes more important.

**Other factors that can accelerate the decision:**
- You need the credibility of a limited company to win larger contracts
- Your clients require you to have limited company status
- You want to retain profits in the business and invest them
- You have a business partner (a partnership or LLP may also be worth considering)

**The 2026 MTD factor:** Making Tax Digital for Income Tax requires sole traders to file quarterly from April 2026 — previously an administrative advantage for limited companies. This partially narrows the admin gap, but limited companies have a separate, more complex compliance regime.

## **Making the switch: what incorporation actually involves**

Incorporating a sole trader business is straightforward but has tax implications worth planning for.

**The process:**
1. Incorporate at Companies House (online, ~£50, same day)
2. Notify HMRC you've ceased self-employment
3. Transfer assets and contracts from sole trader to the company
4. Open a business bank account in the company name
5. Set up payroll for your director's salary
6. Appoint an accountant (strongly recommended)

**Watch out for:**
- **Goodwill and asset transfers** — if you transfer assets of value into the company, HMRC may treat this as a disposal for capital gains purposes
- **Timing** — incorporating mid-year creates complexity; most people choose the start of a new tax year
- **Clients and contracts** — existing contracts may need to be reissued in the company name

The transition typically costs £500–£1,500 in professional fees on top of ongoing accountancy costs. Make sure the long-term tax saving justifies it before you start.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: At what profit level should I become a limited company?**
Most UK accountants now suggest considering incorporation when your consistent annual profit exceeds £40,000–£50,000. Below that level, the tax saving is often outweighed by increased accountancy fees (£800–£2,000/year more) and additional admin. Run the numbers with an accountant before deciding — the break-even point varies depending on your situation.

**Q: Is a limited company better than sole trader for tax?**
Usually yes at higher profit levels, but not automatically. A limited company pays corporation tax (19% up to £50,000) and you pay dividend tax on distributions. A sole trader pays income tax and Class 4 NI. At profits below £35,000–£40,000, the structures are broadly comparable after accounting for the cost of limited company accountancy.

**Q: Does a limited company protect me from personal liability?**
Yes — a limited company is a separate legal entity, so your personal assets are protected if the company has debts or is sued. As a sole trader, you have unlimited personal liability. However, most freelancers and sole traders should have professional indemnity insurance regardless of structure.

**Q: Does Making Tax Digital change the sole trader vs limited company decision?**
MTD for Income Tax (from April 2026) requires sole traders earning over £50,000 to file quarterly — this reduces one of the admin advantages of staying as a sole trader. Limited companies operate under a different, separate regime. MTD doesn't make incorporation more or less attractive on its own, but it's worth factoring into the overall compliance picture.

**Q: How do I switch from sole trader to limited company?**
You incorporate at Companies House (online, ~£50), notify HMRC you've stopped trading as a sole trader, and transfer contracts and assets to the new company. The process is straightforward, but there are tax implications around asset transfers and timing. An accountant can guide you through the transition.
