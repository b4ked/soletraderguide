# **QuickBooks Review 2026: Is It Worth It for UK Sole Traders?**

QuickBooks is one of the most widely-used accounting platforms in the UK. But is it actually a good fit for a sole trader? This review looks at the current offering honestly — pricing, MTD compliance, what it does well, and the one significant gap that affects sole traders specifically.

## **What is QuickBooks?**

QuickBooks is cloud accounting software made by Intuit, a US company with a strong UK presence. It's HMRC-recognised for Making Tax Digital and covers everything from invoicing and expense tracking to bank feeds and basic reporting. It positions itself as suitable for small businesses of all types — but that breadth is both its strength and its weakness for sole traders.

## **Pricing**

QuickBooks UK pricing for sole traders (2025-26):

- **Simple Start:** £14/month + VAT (£16.80/month) — invoicing, bank feeds, MTD, basic reporting
- **Essentials:** £28/month + VAT — adds multi-currency and bill management
- **Plus:** £38/month + VAT — adds project tracking and stock management

Most sole traders need Simple Start only. QuickBooks regularly offers 50% off for the first six months — check the current offer before signing up. The renewal price often surprises people: verify the full-price cost before committing.

There is no free tier and no free-with-banking equivalent (unlike FreeAgent with NatWest/RBS/Mettle).

## **MTD for Income Tax**

QuickBooks is fully HMRC-recognised for Making Tax Digital for Income Tax. It handles digital record keeping and quarterly submissions from within the app. If MTD compliance is your primary need, QuickBooks delivers it cleanly.

The quarterly update workflow is more complex than FreeAgent's — there are more screens to navigate and more accounting terminology in the interface. For a sole trader who just wants to click "submit" each quarter, this can feel like unnecessary friction.

## **The Self Assessment gap**

This is the most important limitation for sole traders. **QuickBooks does not submit Self Assessment tax returns to HMRC.**

FreeAgent handles SA filing directly from within the app. QuickBooks does not. You'll need to:
- Export your profit and loss figures from QuickBooks
- File your Self Assessment separately via HMRC's own portal or separate software
- Or pay an accountant to do it

For a sole trader who wants to manage their own SA return, this is a meaningful gap. For a sole trader who uses an accountant anyway, it matters less.

## **Invoicing and project tracking**

This is where QuickBooks is genuinely strong. The invoicing features are more powerful than FreeAgent's:

- Recurring invoices with automatic payment reminders
- Time tracking linked to invoices (billable hours)
- Project-based income and cost tracking
- Detailed client management

If you invoice regularly with complex billing — different rates, multiple clients per project, time-based billing — QuickBooks handles this better than FreeAgent.

## **Bank feeds and expense capture**

QuickBooks connects to most UK banks via Open Banking. The transaction categorisation is competent and learns your recurring expenses over time. Bank reconciliation is straightforward once set up.

The mobile app is good — expense capture on the go is more polished than FreeAgent's, with a clean receipt photo workflow.

## **Who is QuickBooks best for?**

- Sole traders and freelancers who **invoice frequently** and need strong invoicing features
- Businesses that **track time and bill by the hour**
- Anyone who **already uses an accountant** (who'll handle Self Assessment)
- Businesses planning to **grow and take on staff** (QuickBooks scales better)

**Not ideal for:**
- Sole traders who **file their own Self Assessment** (use FreeAgent instead)
- Those who want the **simplest possible** day-to-day interface
- Anyone with NatWest/RBS/Mettle banking who can get FreeAgent free

## **Verdict**

QuickBooks is solid software, but FreeAgent is the better default for most UK sole traders — primarily because it files Self Assessment directly. The QuickBooks price point is similar to FreeAgent, without that key feature.

Where QuickBooks earns its place: if you do a lot of invoicing, track project hours, or already use an accountant who handles your SA, QuickBooks is worth the money. Simple Start at £16.80/month is competitive, and the invoicing tools are genuinely better.

**Q: Is QuickBooks good for UK sole traders?**

For sole traders who manage their own tax return, FreeAgent is usually the better choice. For those who use an accountant, QuickBooks is a strong option.

**Q: How much does QuickBooks cost for a sole trader in the UK?**
QuickBooks Simple Start costs £14/month plus VAT (£16.80/month). Introductory offers of 50% off are common — check the current deal. There's no free tier, and no free-with-banking option equivalent to FreeAgent with NatWest/RBS.

**Q: Does QuickBooks do Self Assessment for sole traders?**
No. QuickBooks does not file Self Assessment returns to HMRC. You'll need to export your figures and file separately through HMRC's portal, or pay an accountant. FreeAgent handles Self Assessment filing directly, which is a significant advantage for sole traders who file their own returns.

**Q: Is QuickBooks or FreeAgent better for a UK sole trader?**
FreeAgent is better for most sole traders, primarily because it files Self Assessment directly. QuickBooks is better if you have complex invoicing needs, track billable hours, or already use an accountant. If you bank with NatWest, RBS, or Mettle, FreeAgent is free — making it an easy choice.

**Q: Is QuickBooks MTD-compliant for sole traders?**
Yes. QuickBooks is HMRC-recognised for Making Tax Digital for Income Tax and handles quarterly digital submissions. It's a fully compliant option for the April 2026 MTD rollout.
