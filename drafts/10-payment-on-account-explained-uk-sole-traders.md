# **Payment on Account Explained: Why UK Sole Traders Get a Surprise Tax Bill**

Payment on account is one of the most common sources of financial shock for newly self-employed people in the UK. You file your first tax return, expect to pay one bill, and HMRC asks for almost double. Here's exactly how it works and why.

## **What is payment on account?**

Payment on account is HMRC's system of collecting tax in advance. Instead of waiting until January to collect all your tax from the previous year, HMRC also asks you to pay instalments toward your next year's tax bill.

The logic: HMRC wants to collect tax closer to when you earn it, rather than up to 22 months after the fact.

## **How does it work in practice?**

Let's say your tax bill for 2024–25 is £4,000. Here's what HMRC asks you to pay:

●      31 January 2026: £4,000 (balancing payment for 2024–25) + £2,000 (first payment on account for 2025–26)

●      31 July 2026: £2,000 (second payment on account for 2025–26)

●      31 January 2027: balancing payment if your actual 2025–26 bill differs from the advance payments

Each payment on account is 50% of the previous year's total tax bill. So in the first year, you pay your full bill plus an extra 100% on top, spread over two instalments.

## **When does payment on account kick in?**

Payments on account apply if:

●      Your tax bill for the year is more than £1,000, AND

●      Less than 80% of your tax was collected at source (e.g. through PAYE)

If you're employed and only have a small amount of self-employment income, you might not be affected. But most dedicated sole traders will hit this threshold.

## **The first-year shock**

The problem hits hardest in your first full year of self-employment. You've been earning for 12 months, saving what you thought was enough for your tax bill — and then in January you find out you owe not just that bill, but another 50% on top as a prepayment.

This is completely legal and expected by HMRC. It's not a penalty. But it's a cash flow shock that many new sole traders aren't prepared for.

## **How to prepare**

The answer is simple but requires discipline: save more than you think you need.

A common rule of thumb: set aside 25–30% of every payment you receive for tax. In your first year, aim for the higher end of that range to absorb the payment on account.

Some sole traders maintain a separate tax savings account and transfer a percentage of every invoice payment into it automatically.

## **Can you reduce your payments on account?**

Yes. If you know your income will be lower in the next tax year, you can apply to reduce your payments on account. You do this through your HMRC online account or on your Self Assessment tax return.

**Warning:** if you reduce your payments and your actual income turns out to be higher than expected, HMRC charges interest on the underpayment. Only reduce if you're confident your income is genuinely dropping.

Good accounting software like FreeAgent, Xero, or QuickBooks will show you an estimate of your tax liability as the year progresses. This removes the guesswork and means you know roughly what's coming before the January deadline hits.
