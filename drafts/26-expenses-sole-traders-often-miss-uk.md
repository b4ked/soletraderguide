# **Expenses UK Sole Traders Often Miss (And How to Claim Them)**

Most sole traders know the basics: you can deduct business expenses from your income to reduce your tax bill. But there's a gap between "knowing the basics" and actually claiming everything you're entitled to. This post covers the categories most commonly overlooked — and how to claim them correctly.

## **Pre-trading expenses**

Many sole traders miss expenses they incurred before they officially started trading. HMRC allows you to claim pre-trading expenditure — costs you paid **before** your business started that would have been allowable if paid after you started.

This typically includes:
- Laptop or equipment bought in anticipation of starting the business
- Professional training or qualifications directly related to your new trade
- Website or domain costs paid before launch
- Tools or materials bought before first billing a client

The rule: the expense must have been incurred for the purpose of the business and be within **seven years** of when you started trading. Claim these on your first Self Assessment return in the year trading began.

## **The home office: flat rate vs actual costs**

Most home-based sole traders know they can claim home office expenses. Fewer know there are two methods — and many use the wrong one for their situation.

**Flat rate method (simplified expenses):**
HMRC allows you to claim a fixed amount based on hours worked at home per month:
- 25–50 hours/month: £10/month
- 51–100 hours/month: £18/month
- 101+ hours/month: £26/month

This is easy to claim but often undervalues the deduction.

**Actual cost method:**
You calculate the business proportion of actual home costs: rent/mortgage interest, council tax, utilities, broadband. The proportion is typically based on rooms used exclusively for business or hours worked.

For a sole trader who works full-time from home in a three-bed house, the actual cost method often produces a significantly larger deduction — sometimes £1,500–£3,000 per year compared to £312 on the flat rate.

Worth calculating both and using whichever gives the higher figure. The flat rate is simpler; the actual method requires more record-keeping but is more accurate.

## **Equipment: the timing game**

The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) lets you deduct the full cost of business equipment in the year you buy it — not spread over several years. This is powerful for tax planning, but many sole traders miss the timing opportunity.

If you're going to buy equipment (laptop, tools, camera, desk, office furniture), **buying before 5 April** means you get the deduction this tax year. Buying on 6 April means you wait another year.

For a higher rate taxpayer, a £2,000 laptop bought on 4 April vs 6 April is the difference between £800 of tax relief now vs £800 in 12 months. Worth factoring into purchasing decisions.

## **Professional subscriptions and memberships**

Membership fees for professional bodies relevant to your work are fully deductible. Many sole traders either don't claim these or assume they're personal costs.

HMRC maintains an approved list of professional organisations (including many trade bodies, chartered institutes, and professional associations). If your professional body is on the list, annual membership fees are fully deductible. If it's not on HMRC's approved list, you can still claim if the membership is wholly and exclusively for business purposes.

Common examples:
- Membership of a professional institute (CIPD, ICE, CIMA, BCS, etc.)
- Trade association fees
- Industry body subscriptions
- Press passes or industry publication subscriptions used for work

## **Mileage: the full rate, properly tracked**

Many sole traders claim mileage but at the wrong rate or without proper records.

**HMRC approved mileage rates (2025-26):**
- Cars and vans: 45p per mile for first 10,000 miles, 25p per mile above that
- Motorcycles: 24p per mile
- Bicycles: 20p per mile

These rates are designed to cover fuel, wear and tear, and depreciation. Many people claim mileage but only claim a petrol cost figure — missing the full deduction.

**Records required:** date, journey destination, business purpose, miles. HMRC can reject mileage claims without adequate records. A mileage log app or even a simple spreadsheet updated after each journey is sufficient.

Journeys to a regular, fixed workplace don't qualify (that's commuting). But journeys to client sites, meetings, events, and different work locations do.

## **Training and professional development**

Training expenses are deductible if they update or improve skills used in your existing trade. Many sole traders don't claim these — or claim them inconsistently.

**Claimable:**
- A copywriter updating their SEO skills
- An electrician taking a new safety certification
- A designer learning a new tool they already use professionally

**Not claimable:**
- Training that sets you up for an entirely new career or trade
- Undergraduate or postgraduate degrees (generally not allowable, with narrow exceptions)

The test: does it maintain or improve skills in your current trade? If yes, claim it.

## **Bank charges and finance costs**

If you have a dedicated business bank account, monthly account fees are deductible. Many sole traders overlook these because they're small and automatic.

Also claimable:
- Credit card processing fees (Stripe, SumUp, PayPal fees on business transactions)
- Foreign exchange transaction fees on business payments
- Interest on a business loan (not on personal debt used for business — only specifically business borrowing)

## **Phone and broadband: the actual use proportion**

If you use a personal phone or home broadband for business, you can claim the business proportion — not the full cost.

A practical approach:
- Estimate the percentage of use that's business (e.g., 40% of calls are business)
- Claim that percentage of your monthly bill

If you have a separate business phone or SIM, 100% of the cost is deductible.

Broadband: if you work from home and use broadband for business, a reasonable proportion (often 50–80% if you work primarily from home) can be claimed. The actual cost method here connects to the wider home office calculation.

## **The mental model: wholly and exclusively**

The rule underlying all allowable expenses is that they must be "wholly and exclusively" for business purposes. This doesn't mean 100% of your life must be business — it means the expense was incurred for the business, not for personal reasons.

When in doubt, ask: "Would I have incurred this expense if I didn't have the business?" If no, it's probably claimable. If you're mixing personal and business use, you can claim the business proportion.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: Can I claim expenses I paid before I started my business?**
Yes. HMRC allows pre-trading expenditure — costs incurred up to seven years before you started trading, provided they would have been allowable if paid after you started. Common examples include equipment, training, and website costs paid before your first client. Claim them on your first Self Assessment return.

**Q: Should I use the flat rate or actual costs for my home office?**
It depends on your situation. The flat rate (up to £26/month) is simpler but often produces a smaller deduction. If you work full-time from home, calculating the actual business proportion of your rent/mortgage, council tax, and utilities usually gives a larger deduction. Calculate both and use whichever is higher.

**Q: Can I claim professional membership fees as a sole trader?**
Yes, if the membership is relevant to your trade. HMRC maintains an approved list of professional bodies whose fees are automatically allowable. For other organisations, the fee is deductible if the membership is wholly and exclusively for business purposes. Trade body fees and chartered institute memberships are common examples.

**Q: Can I claim training costs as a sole trader?**
Yes, if the training maintains or improves skills in your existing trade. Training to update your skills (a designer learning a new tool, an accountant taking a CPD course) is allowable. Training to move into a completely new career or trade is not. The key distinction is whether it builds on your current trade or starts a new one.

**Q: Can I claim the full HMRC mileage rate if I use my personal car for business?**
Yes. If you use your own vehicle for business journeys, you can claim 45p per mile for the first 10,000 miles and 25p per mile above that. This rate covers fuel, wear, and depreciation — you don't claim actual fuel separately. You must keep records of each journey (date, destination, business purpose, miles).
