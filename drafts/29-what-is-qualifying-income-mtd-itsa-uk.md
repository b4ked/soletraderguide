# **What Is Qualifying Income for Making Tax Digital? (MTD ITSA Explained)**

The Making Tax Digital threshold — £50,000 from April 2026 — sounds simple until you ask what actually counts towards it. Rental income and self-employment income are both included. PAYE salary is not. Savings interest and dividends are not. The answer depends on where your income comes from — and the detail matters for many people sitting near the threshold.

## **What MTD ITSA is and what triggers it**

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment (MTD ITSA) is a new system that replaces the annual Self Assessment return for people with certain types of income above a threshold.

The thresholds:
- **£50,000+:** Mandatory from April 2026
- **£30,000+:** Mandatory from April 2027
- **£20,000+:** Mandatory from April 2028

The question for most people is: which of my income sources are added together to calculate whether I'm above the threshold?

## **What counts as qualifying income**

HMRC's qualifying income for MTD ITSA includes **two categories only:**

### 1. Self-employment income (gross turnover)
This is your **gross trading income** — what you invoice or earn from your business **before** deducting any expenses.

- If you're a freelancer or sole trader, it's your total invoiced amount (or total revenue received)
- It is **not** your profit
- All business activities count — if you have multiple self-employed trades, the gross income from each is combined

**Example:** A graphic designer with £48,000 in client invoices and £30,000 in business expenses has a gross self-employment income of £48,000 — below the £50,000 threshold. Their profit (£18,000) is not the figure used.

### 2. UK property income (gross rental income)
If you receive rental income from UK property, the **gross rent** you receive (before any property expenses) counts towards your qualifying income for MTD.

- This includes residential lettings, holiday lets, and commercial property
- It does **not** include income from overseas property (this falls under different rules)
- Rent-a-room income counts (gross, before the rent-a-room allowance is applied)

**Example:** A sole trader with £35,000 in business income and £18,000 in rental income has qualifying income of £53,000 — above the £50,000 threshold, so they must comply from April 2026.

## **What does NOT count as qualifying income**

The following income types do **not** count towards the MTD ITSA qualifying income threshold:

| Income type | Counts for MTD threshold? |
|---|---|
| Self-employment (gross) | Yes |
| UK property income (gross) | Yes |
| PAYE employment income | No |
| Pension income | No |
| UK savings interest | No |
| Dividends | No |
| Capital gains | No |
| Overseas property income | No |

This distinction catches many people out. Someone earning £60,000 from PAYE employment and £10,000 from self-employment has qualifying income of £10,000 — well below the threshold, even though their total income is high.

## **Common scenarios explained**

### Sole trader only
A freelance photographer with gross client income of £52,000 and business costs of £12,000 (net profit £40,000) has **qualifying income of £52,000** — above the threshold. MTD applies from April 2026.

### Sole trader + PAYE employment
A part-time employee earning £25,000 from an employer and £28,000 from freelance work has qualifying income of **£28,000** (the self-employment gross income only). Below the £50,000 threshold in 2026; may come in scope at the £30,000 threshold in 2027.

### Sole trader + UK property
A self-employed consultant earning £40,000 in gross consultancy fees who also receives £12,000 in annual rent has qualifying income of **£52,000** — combined self-employment and property income. Above the £50,000 threshold for April 2026.

### Property only
A landlord who has no self-employment income but receives £55,000 gross rental income from multiple properties has qualifying income of **£55,000**. MTD applies from April 2026.

### PAYE employment + property
An employee earning £80,000 from their employer and £18,000 in rent from a buy-to-let property has qualifying income of **£18,000** (rental income only; PAYE is excluded). Below all current thresholds.

## **Gross income vs net income: why it matters**

The threshold is assessed on **gross income**, not profit. This is deliberate — it captures people by business activity level, not after-expense profitability.

For sole traders with high costs (materials, subcontractors, studio space), gross income can significantly exceed profit. A builder with £80,000 in turnover and £50,000 in costs has gross qualifying income of £80,000 — fully in scope — despite a profit of only £30,000.

For landlords, mortgage interest and maintenance costs are excluded from the threshold calculation. A landlord with £55,000 gross rent and £30,000 in costs still has £55,000 qualifying income.

## **Checking whether you're in scope**

Look at your most recent Self Assessment return:
- Your gross self-employment income is shown on the self-employment pages (turnover, before expenses)
- Your gross rental income is shown on the UK property pages (total income received, before expenses)
- Add these two figures together

If the total is above £50,000, you're in scope for MTD from April 2026.

If it's between £30,000 and £50,000, you come in scope from April 2027.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: Does PAYE salary count towards the MTD qualifying income threshold?**
No. PAYE employment income is excluded from the MTD ITSA qualifying income calculation. Only gross self-employment income and gross UK property income count towards the threshold. A high-earning employee is only in scope for MTD if they also have significant self-employment or rental income above the threshold.

**Q: Does rental income count for Making Tax Digital?**
Yes. Gross UK rental income (before property expenses) counts towards the MTD ITSA qualifying income threshold. If your gross rent plus gross self-employment income together exceed £50,000, you must comply with MTD ITSA from April 2026. Overseas property income does not count.

**Q: Is the MTD threshold based on income or profit?**
Gross income, not profit. Your qualifying income is your total self-employment turnover and gross rental income before any expenses are deducted. This means sole traders with high costs can still be well above the threshold even with modest profits.

**Q: What if my income varies year to year — which year is used?**
HMRC uses the income figure from the most recently filed Self Assessment return to assess whether you need to comply with MTD. If your income in one year takes you above the threshold, you'll be required to comply from the following April. If your income subsequently falls below the threshold, you may be able to exit MTD, but you must contact HMRC.

**Q: Do savings interest and dividends count towards the MTD threshold?**
No. Savings interest and dividends are excluded from qualifying income for MTD ITSA. These income sources still need to be declared on your tax return, but they do not count towards the £50,000 threshold.
