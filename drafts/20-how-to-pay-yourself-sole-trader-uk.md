# **How to Pay Yourself as a UK Sole Trader (And How Much to Keep Back for Tax)**

One of the most common questions new freelancers and sole traders ask is: "So how do I actually pay myself?" It's a reasonable question — if you're used to receiving a salary, the way a sole trader takes money feels different and slightly odd at first. This guide explains exactly how it works, how much you can take, and how to make sure you don't spend your tax bill.

## **The basics: drawings, not salary**

As a sole trader, you don't pay yourself a salary. You aren't an employee of your business — you *are* your business. Instead, you take money from your business bank account as and when you need it. These withdrawals are called **drawings** (or owner's drawings).

There is no HMRC approval required. No payroll. No PAYE. You simply transfer money from your business account to your personal account.

**The critical thing to understand:** HMRC taxes you on your **profit**, not on how much you withdraw. Whether you draw £2,000 a month or leave everything in the business account until December, your tax bill is calculated the same way — on your total profit for the tax year.

This means you can't reduce your tax bill by taking less out of your business. But it also means your drawings don't trigger any additional tax event.

## **How much can you pay yourself?**

There's no legal minimum or maximum for sole trader drawings. You can take as much or as little as your cash flow allows.

**Practical framework:**

1. Calculate your expected annual profit (income minus allowable expenses)
2. Estimate your tax bill (roughly 20–30% of profit for most sole traders — use software for accuracy)
3. Set aside that amount as "HMRC's money" in a separate account or pot
4. The remaining profit is yours to draw as you need it

**Example — profit of £40,000:**
- Estimated income tax: ~£5,486 (20% on profit above £12,570)
- Class 4 NI: ~£1,647 (6% on £27,430)
- Total to set aside: ~£7,133 (~18% of profit)
- Available to draw: ~£32,867

Most sole traders set aside **25–30%** as a safe buffer — this gives a cushion for payment on account and any underestimates.

## **Setting up a tax savings system**

The biggest financial mistake new sole traders make is treating all income as available income. By January, the tax money is gone.

**The system that works:**

1. **Open a dedicated savings account** — or use a named "pot" or "space" in digital banking apps like Monzo, Starling, or Mettle. Label it "Tax" and treat it as untouchable.
2. **Transfer on the same day as every client payment** — 25–30% of whatever lands goes straight to the tax pot. Don't wait until month-end.
3. **Never dip into it** — this money isn't yours. It belongs to HMRC. Mentally ring-fence it from the day it's transferred.
4. **Review quarterly** — when you do your MTD quarterly update (from April 2026 if earnings are over £50k), check your tax estimate and adjust your saving rate if your income has changed.

## **Payment on account: why January is often bigger than you expect**

Once your tax bill exceeds £1,000, HMRC requires you to make advance payments toward the *next* year's tax bill — called payments on account. This catches many sole traders off guard.

**What happens in January, year two:**
- You pay your balancing payment for last year (what you owe)
- *Plus* a first payment on account for this year (50% of last year's bill)

So if your tax bill is £6,000, your January payment is potentially £9,000 (£6,000 + £3,000). Followed by a further £3,000 in July.

Saving 25–30% from the start — not just 20% — helps absorb this.

## **Should you use pension contributions to reduce your tax?**

Yes — and it's one of the most effective tax planning tools available to sole traders.

Pension contributions reduce your **adjusted net income**, which directly reduces your income tax bill:

- Basic rate taxpayer (under £50,270): every £1 contributed to a pension saves 20p in tax (25% effective return on contributions)
- Higher rate taxpayer (£50,270–£125,140): every £1 saves 40p — claim the extra relief through Self Assessment
- Income between £100,000–£125,140: every £1 saves up to 60p (due to the Personal Allowance trap)

**The annual pension allowance is £60,000** (or 100% of your net relevant earnings, whichever is lower). Unused allowance can be carried forward from the previous three years.

For a sole trader earning £50,000, making a £5,000 pension contribution saves approximately £2,000 in tax. That money goes into your retirement pot rather than to HMRC.

## **Seasonal income: smoothing your drawings**

If your income is lumpy — some months strong, some quiet — managing drawings requires more thought.

**Practical approaches:**
- Pay yourself a consistent monthly amount based on your *expected annual profit*, not your month-to-month income
- Keep a cash buffer in your business account (one to two months of your typical drawings) to smooth out dry periods
- In strong months, direct the surplus to your tax pot first, then your personal buffer
- Avoid the temptation to inflate drawings in good months — the tax on a bumper year is paid the following January

## **Record-keeping: tracking your drawings**

HMRC doesn't require you to document every drawing in the same way you document expenses — drawings aren't an expense and they don't appear on your Self Assessment return. However, keeping a simple log is good practice:

- Record all bank transfers between your business and personal accounts
- Your accounting software will show these as "drawings" or "owner's equity" movements
- This is useful if HMRC ever queries your bank statements

The real record-keeping requirement is on your income and expenses — not on how you pay yourself. Keep invoices, receipts, and bank records for at least 5 years.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: How do I pay myself as a sole trader?**
You take drawings — a simple bank transfer from your business account to your personal account. There's no payroll, no PAYE, and no approval required. You can take money as and when you need it. HMRC taxes you on your annual profit regardless of how much you withdraw, so drawings don't affect your tax bill.

**Q: Can a sole trader pay themselves a salary?**
No — sole traders can't put themselves on payroll. You take drawings from business profit instead. If you want to pay yourself via PAYE payroll, you would need to operate as a limited company and become a director-employee of that company. As a sole trader, the concept of a "salary" simply doesn't exist.

**Q: How much should I pay myself as a sole trader?**
Pay yourself what you can afford after setting aside 25–30% for tax. A practical approach: estimate your annual profit, calculate your likely tax bill (or use accounting software to track it), set that aside in a dedicated savings account, and draw the remainder as needed. Don't treat all revenue as income — a chunk always belongs to HMRC.

**Q: How much should I keep back for tax as a sole trader?**
Set aside 25–30% of every client payment into a separate tax savings account. This covers income tax, Class 4 National Insurance, and provides a buffer for the payment on account system. If your profit is likely to exceed £50,270 (higher rate threshold), save closer to 35–40%.

**Q: Does making pension contributions reduce how much tax I pay as a sole trader?**
Yes. Pension contributions reduce your adjusted net income, which directly reduces your tax bill. Basic rate taxpayers save 20p per £1 contributed; higher rate taxpayers save 40p. For sole traders earning between £100,000 and £125,140, where the effective marginal rate is 60%, pension contributions are particularly powerful.
