# **National Insurance for UK Sole Traders: Class 2 and Class 4 Explained (2025-26)**

If you're self-employed in the UK, the rules around National Insurance changed significantly in April 2024 — and a lot of sole traders still don't have a clear picture of what they actually owe. This guide covers exactly what you need to know for 2025-26: what Class 2 and Class 4 mean, what rates apply, and how it all connects to your State Pension.

## **What National Insurance do sole traders pay?**

As a sole trader, you are responsible for two types of National Insurance:

- **Class 2 NI** — a flat-rate contribution (historically £3.45/week). As of April 2024, this is no longer compulsory for most sole traders. It still exists as a voluntary contribution.
- **Class 4 NI** — a percentage of your profits, calculated on your Self Assessment return. This is still mandatory if your profits exceed the threshold.

Class 1 National Insurance — the kind deducted from employees' salaries — does not apply to sole traders for their self-employment income.

## **Class 2 National Insurance: Abolished (But Not Gone)**

In April 2024, the government abolished compulsory Class 2 National Insurance for self-employed people. This was a meaningful saving — previously, most sole traders paid approximately £180/year in Class 2 contributions regardless of profits.

**What this means now:**

- Sole traders with profits above £12,570 automatically receive a qualifying year for their State Pension — **no Class 2 payment required**.
- Class 2 still exists but is now **optional**. You can pay £3.50/week voluntarily.
- Sole traders with profits between £6,845 (the Small Profits Threshold) and £12,570 do **not** automatically get a qualifying State Pension year. If this applies to you, voluntary Class 2 contributions are still worth considering.

**The bottom line:** if your profits are above £12,570, you don't need to do anything about Class 2. Your State Pension entitlement is protected automatically.

## **Class 4 National Insurance: Rates and Thresholds for 2025-26**

Class 4 NI is the main National Insurance charge for sole traders. It's calculated as a percentage of your taxable profit and collected via Self Assessment.

**2025-26 Class 4 rates:**

| Profit band | Rate |
|---|---|
| Below £12,570 | 0% |
| £12,570 to £50,270 | 6% |
| Above £50,270 | 2% |

**Example — profit of £35,000:**
- £35,000 − £12,570 = £22,430 subject to Class 4
- £22,430 × 6% = **£1,345.80 Class 4 NI due**

Note: these rates were reduced in 2024 (down from 9% on the main band). A sole trader earning £35,000 saves approximately £335/year compared to 2023-24 rates.

## **When and how do you pay your National Insurance?**

Class 4 NI is calculated and paid as part of your Self Assessment tax return — you don't pay it separately. HMRC calculates it from your profit figures and includes it in your January payment.

**Payment timeline:**
- **31 January** — First payment on account (NI included) + balancing payment from previous year
- **31 July** — Second payment on account
- Class 4 NI is subject to the same payment on account system as income tax

Your accounting software (FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks) will show you a running estimate of your combined tax and NI liability throughout the year.

## **National Insurance and your State Pension**

This is the part that changed most significantly in April 2024 and that many sole traders are still confused about.

**Before April 2024:** you needed to pay Class 2 NI to qualify for a State Pension year.

**From April 2024:** if your profits are above £12,570, you automatically receive a qualifying year — whether or not you make any NI payment. Your State Pension entitlement builds up through Class 4 contributions alone.

You need 35 qualifying years of NI contributions for the full new State Pension (currently £230.25/week). If you're concerned about gaps in your NI record, check your forecast at gov.uk — this is particularly important if you have years of low earnings, career breaks, or are new to self-employment.

## **What if you're both employed and self-employed?**

If you have both employment income and self-employment income:

- **Class 1 NI** is deducted from your employment salary by your employer
- **Class 4 NI** is calculated on your self-employment profits via Self Assessment

Both apply. However, there is a mechanism to limit Class 4 contributions if your Class 1 contributions from employment already reach the annual maximum. This most commonly affects people whose combined income is above £50,270. If your combined income is high, it's worth reviewing this with an accountant to avoid overpayment.

## **Small profits: do you owe anything?**

If your sole trader profits are below **£6,845** (the Small Profits Threshold for 2025-26), you don't owe any NI at all.

However, you also won't automatically get a qualifying year for your State Pension. If you want to protect your pension record, you can pay voluntary Class 2 NI at £3.50/week — that's £182/year for a full qualifying year. For most people with low profits, this is worth doing.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: Do UK sole traders still pay Class 2 National Insurance?**
No — Class 2 NI was abolished for most sole traders in April 2024. If your profits are above £12,570, you automatically get a qualifying year for your State Pension without any Class 2 payment. Class 2 still exists as a voluntary contribution (£3.50/week) for those with profits between £6,845 and £12,570 who want to protect their State Pension record.

**Q: How much Class 4 National Insurance do sole traders pay in 2025-26?**
Class 4 NI is 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, and 2% on profits above £50,270. It's calculated on your Self Assessment return and paid alongside your income tax. A sole trader with £35,000 profit pays approximately £1,345 in Class 4 NI.

**Q: When do sole traders pay National Insurance?**
Class 4 NI is included in your Self Assessment payments: 50% on 31 January (with your first payment on account), and 50% on 31 July (second payment on account). You don't pay NI separately — it's calculated and collected through the same system as income tax.

**Q: Does National Insurance affect my State Pension as a sole trader?**
From April 2024, sole traders with profits above £12,570 automatically receive a qualifying year for the State Pension — you don't need to pay Class 2 separately. You need 35 qualifying years for the full new State Pension. Check your NI record at gov.uk to see where you stand.

**Q: If I'm employed and self-employed, do I pay National Insurance twice?**
In a sense, yes. You pay Class 1 NI on your employment income and Class 4 NI on your self-employment profits. However, if your combined income is high, there's a mechanism to cap your total NI contributions. This is worth reviewing with an accountant if your combined income exceeds £50,270.
