# **IR35 Explained for Sole Traders: Do You Need to Worry About It?**

IR35 is one of those tax terms that causes a lot of anxiety among self-employed people in the UK — often because it's poorly explained. This guide breaks it down in plain English and tells you honestly whether it's something you need to worry about.

## **What is IR35?**

IR35 is a set of HMRC rules designed to stop "disguised employment" — the practice of someone working essentially as an employee but being paid as a contractor to avoid income tax and National Insurance.

The name comes from the Inland Revenue press release that announced it: IR35, published in 1999. It's been a source of controversy ever since.

## **Does IR35 apply to sole traders?**

Here's the part that surprises many people: IR35 technically applies to personal service companies (Ltd companies), not directly to sole traders.

As a sole trader, you don't have a company between you and your client. You're personally contracted to do work and you pay income tax and Class 4 National Insurance on your profits through Self Assessment. There's no company structure to "disguise" employment through.

So in the strict legal sense, IR35 as legislation doesn't apply to sole traders.

## **But there's still a risk — employed vs self-employed status**

Just because IR35 doesn't technically apply doesn't mean HMRC won't scrutinise your working arrangements. HMRC can investigate whether you're genuinely self-employed or whether you should be classified as an employee of your client.

If HMRC decides you're actually employed (not genuinely self-employed), they can demand the income tax and National Insurance that should have been paid under PAYE. This can go back years and include penalties.

## **The key tests HMRC uses**

HMRC looks at several factors to determine whether someone is genuinely self-employed:

●      [object Object]Can you send someone else to do the work in your place? Genuine self-employment usually means yes.

●      [object Object]Does the client tell you how, when, and where to work? Heavy client control suggests employment.

●      [object Object]Is the client obliged to offer you work and are you obliged to accept it? That's employment-like.

●      [object Object]Do you risk your own money? Do you quote for jobs and bear the cost if something goes wrong? Self-employed people typically do.

●      [object Object]Are you treated like a member of staff — with a company email, attending internal meetings, following staff policies?

## **Who is most at risk?**

Sole traders most at risk of HMRC questioning their employment status are those who:

●      Have only one client (especially long-term)

●      Work exclusively at the client's premises

●      Are supervised and directed by the client day to day

●      Don't invoice — the client just pays a regular amount

●      Work the same hours as the client's employees

## **How to protect yourself**

The key is to operate in a way that clearly demonstrates genuine self-employment:

●      Have multiple clients where possible

●      Use your own equipment

●      Have a proper written contract that reflects self-employment

●      Issue invoices for your work

●      Have your own business insurance

●      Set your own hours and working methods where possible

●      Include a substitution clause in your contracts (even if you'd rarely use it)

## **HMRC's CEST tool**

HMRC offers a free tool called Check Employment Status for Tax (CEST) at gov.uk. You can run through your working arrangements and get HMRC's view on whether you're employed or self-employed.

CEST isn't perfect — it's been criticised for oversimplifying — but using it and keeping the result is useful evidence if HMRC ever questions you.

## **The bottom line**

If you're a sole trader doing genuinely self-employed work — multiple clients, your own methods, financial risk, proper contracts — you have very little to worry about. IR35 itself doesn't apply to you, and HMRC's employed vs self-employed tests are fairly clear.

If you have one long-term client and operate essentially like an employee, it's worth reviewing your working arrangements. A good accountant or the CEST tool can give you clarity.
