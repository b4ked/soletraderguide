# **Allowable Expenses for UK Sole Traders: The Complete HMRC List (2026)**

Claiming allowable expenses is one of the most straightforward ways to reduce your tax bill as a sole trader. Every pound you legitimately claim as a business expense reduces your taxable profit by a pound — meaning you pay less income tax and less Class 4 National Insurance.

This guide covers every expense category HMRC allows, with practical guidance on what qualifies.

## **The golden rule**

HMRC allows expenses that are "wholly and exclusively" for business purposes. If something has a personal element as well as a business element, you can usually only claim the business proportion.

## **Office costs**

If you work from home, you can claim a proportion of your home running costs — heating, electricity, broadband, and even rent or mortgage interest in some cases.

**Simplified flat rate (easiest):** HMRC allows a flat rate based on hours worked from home: £10/month for 25–50 hours, £18/month for 51–100 hours, £26/month for 101+ hours. No receipts needed.

**Actual costs (more accurate):** Calculate the business proportion of your actual home costs. For example, if your home has 5 rooms and you use one exclusively for work, you can claim 1/5 of relevant costs.

If you rent office space separately, the full cost is claimable.

## **Travel costs**

●      Travel to client sites, meetings, and business locations is claimable

●      HMRC mileage rate for cars: 45p per mile for the first 10,000 miles, 25p per mile after

●      Public transport, taxis, parking costs for business journeys

●      Train tickets for business travel

- **Not claimable:** commuting to a regular place of work

## **Staff costs**

If you employ staff or use subcontractors, their wages, salaries, and National Insurance contributions are claimable. Keep payslips, contracts, and payment records.

## **Professional fees**

Accountant fees, bookkeeper fees, legal fees for business matters, professional membership fees relevant to your trade — all claimable. Your annual subscription to accounting software is claimable here too.

## **Marketing and advertising**

●      Website costs — hosting, domain, design

●      Online advertising (Google, Facebook, LinkedIn ads)

●      Business cards, leaflets, printed materials

●      Sponsorships directly related to your business

●      Photography for business use

## **Equipment and technology**

Computers, phones, cameras, tools, and other equipment used for your business. If the item is also used personally, claim only the business proportion.

Under the Annual Investment Allowance, you can deduct the full cost of qualifying equipment in the year of purchase rather than depreciating it over time.

## **Phone and internet**

Business calls and a proportion of your broadband costs. If your phone is used for both personal and business use, calculate and claim only the business proportion (typically 50–80% for most self-employed people).

## **Training and development**

Training courses that update or improve skills you already use in your business are claimable. Training to learn a completely new trade is generally not claimable.

Books, subscriptions, and online courses related to your existing work qualify.

## **Financial costs**

●      Bank charges on your business account

●      Interest on business loans

●      Business insurance (professional indemnity, public liability, etc.)

- **Not claimable:** personal bank account fees, personal loans

## **Food and entertainment**

This is where many sole traders get it wrong. You generally cannot claim food or entertainment expenses — HMRC's "wholly and exclusively" rule rules out meals that would otherwise be personal expenditure.

**Exception:** if you're travelling away from your normal work base overnight for business and incur additional costs, reasonable meal expenses on those trips can be claimable.

Client entertainment is explicitly not allowed as a deductible expense for income tax purposes.

## **Clothing**

Work clothing is only claimable if it is a uniform, protective clothing, or a costume that you couldn't wear in everyday life. A suit you also wear to weddings is not claimable, even if you bought it for a business meeting.

## **Keeping records**

HMRC requires you to keep records of all business expenses for at least 5 years after the Self Assessment deadline for that tax year. Digital records — photos of receipts, accounting software exports — are acceptable.

Use accounting software like FreeAgent, Xero, or QuickBooks to capture expenses as you go rather than scrambling at tax return time.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: What expenses can a UK sole trader claim?**
UK sole traders can claim expenses that are wholly and exclusively for business use: office costs (including home office), travel to client sites, equipment, phone and internet (business proportion), professional fees, marketing, training relevant to your current trade, and business insurance. Personal costs are not claimable.

**Q: Can I claim my home broadband as a sole trader expense?**
Yes, but only the business proportion. If you use broadband for work and personal use, calculate roughly what percentage is for work and claim that portion. Most sole traders claim 50–80% depending on their usage.

**Q: What is the HMRC mileage rate for sole traders?**
HMRC allows 45p per mile for the first 10,000 business miles in a car, and 25p per mile after that. Motorbikes are 24p per mile, and bicycles 20p per mile. Keep a mileage log with the date, purpose, and distance of each journey.

**Q: Can I claim clothing as a sole trader business expense?**
Only if it is a uniform, protective clothing, or a costume you could not wear in everyday life. A suit worn to client meetings is not claimable, even if you bought it specifically for work. HMRC's "wholly and exclusively" rule applies strictly to clothing.

**Q: How long do I need to keep expense records as a sole trader?**
HMRC requires you to keep records for at least 5 years after the Self Assessment filing deadline for that tax year. Digital records — photos of receipts or accounting software exports — are acceptable.
