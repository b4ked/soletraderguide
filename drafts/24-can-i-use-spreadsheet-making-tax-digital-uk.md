# **Can I Use a Spreadsheet for Making Tax Digital? What HMRC Allows**

Many sole traders have tracked their income and expenses in a spreadsheet for years. When Making Tax Digital arrives, do you have to switch to new software? Or can you keep using Excel or Google Sheets?

The short answer: you can keep using a spreadsheet, but you'll need bridging software to actually submit your quarterly updates to HMRC. Here's what that means in practice.

## **What HMRC requires for MTD**

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (MTD ITSA) requires sole traders to:

1. Keep **digital records** of all business income and expenses
2. Submit **quarterly updates** to HMRC through HMRC-recognised software
3. Submit an **end-of-period statement** and final declaration at year end

A spreadsheet (Excel, Google Sheets, Numbers) satisfies requirement 1 — your records are digital. But a spreadsheet alone cannot submit to HMRC. It has no API connection to HMRC's systems. This is where bridging software comes in.

## **What is MTD bridging software?**

Bridging software sits between your spreadsheet and HMRC. You continue recording income and expenses in your spreadsheet as normal. When it's time to submit a quarterly update, the bridging software:

1. Reads the summary figures from your spreadsheet
2. Formats them in the required digital format
3. Submits them to HMRC via the MTD API

You don't change how you run your spreadsheet — you just add the bridging step at submission time.

**Examples of MTD bridging software that works with spreadsheets:**
- **Absolute Bridging** — simple, dedicated bridging tool
- **BTCSoftware** — used by accountants and sole traders
- **Xero** — can act as a bridge if you don't want full accounting features
- **DataDear** — Excel add-in that connects directly to HMRC

Pricing varies but is typically £10–£30/year for a sole trader, significantly cheaper than full accounting software.

## **HMRC's digital links requirement**

There's an important detail: HMRC requires "digital links" throughout your record-keeping. This means data must flow digitally from your records to your submission — you cannot manually type figures from your spreadsheet into another piece of software. Copy-paste is also not permitted.

In practice, this means:
- Your bridging software must read your spreadsheet data directly (via a file export, API connection, or add-in)
- You cannot total your figures on paper and then type them into the bridge

This rules out some informal workarounds. Check that any bridging software you use maintains a full digital link from your spreadsheet records to the HMRC submission.

## **Spreadsheet + bridge: when it works well**

The spreadsheet-plus-bridge approach works best for:

- Sole traders with **simple, low-volume transactions** (few clients, predictable income)
- Those who already have a **well-organised spreadsheet** they're comfortable with
- Anyone who wants the **lowest possible cost** for MTD compliance
- Sole traders who do **most of their own bookkeeping** and just need the HMRC connection

If your spreadsheet already captures income and expenses clearly, the bridging software just adds the submission step. Annual cost: possibly under £30.

## **When full accounting software is better**

A dedicated accounting package (FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks, Sage) handles everything in one place — records, bank feeds, quarterly submissions, and Self Assessment. Consider switching if:

- Your transaction volume is **high or growing** — manual spreadsheet entry becomes time-consuming
- You want **automatic bank feeds** (no manual data entry)
- You need to **file Self Assessment** from within the software (FreeAgent does this; spreadsheets don't)
- You want a **running tax estimate** throughout the year
- You find the bridging process **adds friction** you'd rather eliminate

The cost difference between bridging software (~£25/year) and accounting software (~£200–£300/year) is real. But the time saving and reduced errors can justify the upgrade for many sole traders.

## **The honest recommendation**

If you're well-organised, have low transaction volumes, and your spreadsheet works for you — continue using it with bridging software. There's no obligation to switch to full accounting software. MTD does not require it.

If your spreadsheet is already a source of stress, or you're approaching the higher rate threshold and want better visibility of your tax position throughout the year, the switch to accounting software is worth the cost.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: Can I use Excel or Google Sheets for Making Tax Digital?**
Yes, but not on their own. Spreadsheets satisfy HMRC's digital record-keeping requirement, but you need bridging software to actually submit your quarterly updates to HMRC. Bridging software connects your spreadsheet to HMRC's MTD systems. It typically costs £10–£30/year.

**Q: What is MTD bridging software?**
Bridging software sits between your spreadsheet and HMRC. You continue recording income and expenses in your spreadsheet; when a quarterly update is due, the bridging software reads your figures and submits them to HMRC digitally. It maintains the "digital link" HMRC requires.

**Q: Do I have to switch from my spreadsheet to accounting software for MTD?**
No. Using a spreadsheet with compatible bridging software is a fully HMRC-compliant approach to MTD. You're not required to use dedicated accounting software. Many sole traders with simple finances will find the spreadsheet-plus-bridge approach cheaper and sufficient.

**Q: What are HMRC's digital links rules?**
HMRC requires that data flows digitally from your records to your MTD submission — you can't manually type figures from one place to another. Your bridging software must read your spreadsheet data directly (via export or add-in). Copy-paste and manual re-entry are not permitted.

**Q: What's cheaper — bridging software or full accounting software?**
Bridging software is significantly cheaper, typically £10–£30/year compared to £200–£350/year for full accounting software. The trade-off is that bridging software only handles the HMRC submission — it doesn't give you bank feeds, running tax estimates, Self Assessment filing, or automated categorisation.
