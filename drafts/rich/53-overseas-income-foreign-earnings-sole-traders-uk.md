## The short version

If you're a UK tax resident, HMRC taxes you on your worldwide income. That includes every invoice you send to a client in the US, Germany, Australia, or anywhere else.

The currency doesn't change the liability. The client's location doesn't change the liability. If money came in for work you did, it goes on your return — converted to pounds sterling.

That said, the rules around foreign income are manageable once you understand three things: how to record it, how to convert it, and how to avoid being taxed twice through double taxation relief.

---

## Worldwide income: what it means in practice

UK tax residency is the trigger. If you're UK tax resident — which most people living and working in the UK are — you pay UK income tax on income from everywhere.

This catches out sole traders who assume that invoicing in dollars or euros somehow keeps the money "outside" the UK system. It doesn't.

What matters:
- Where you are tax resident (UK = worldwide income is taxable)
- What the income is for (self-employment, employment, property, investments)
- Whether a double taxation agreement applies

What doesn't matter:
- Where your client is based
- What currency you were paid in
- Whether the money went into a foreign bank account first

## Recording foreign currency income

Every invoice you raise to a foreign client needs to be recorded in pounds sterling in your accounts.

HMRC accepts two exchange rate approaches:

| Method | How it works | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Actual rate at transaction date | Use the exchange rate on the date you received payment | Those with few foreign transactions or large irregular payments |
| HMRC annual average rate | Use HMRC's published average rate for the tax year | Traders with regular, recurring foreign income |
| Bank rate | Use the rate your bank applied when converting the payment | Acceptable if conversion happened at the bank |

HMRC publishes average exchange rates annually on GOV.UK. These are straightforward to apply and are widely accepted. You cannot use different methods for different transactions in the same year — pick one approach and apply it consistently.

## Worked example

**The situation:** Rachel is a UK-based freelance web developer. In January 2026, she completes a project for a US client and is paid $10,000.

**The conversion:** The exchange rate on the date she receives the payment is 1.27 (£1 = $1.27).

**The calculation:**

$10,000 ÷ 1.27 = **£7,874**

Rachel records £7,874 as business income in her accounts. She reports it on her Self Assessment return under self-employment income. It's taxed the same way as any other business income — after deducting allowable expenses, and subject to her personal allowance and income tax rates.

**If she uses HMRC's annual average rate instead:** HMRC published an average USD/GBP rate of 1.285 for 2025/26 (illustrative figure). She'd record $10,000 ÷ 1.285 = £7,782. The difference is small for a single invoice — and using the average rate saves her looking up individual rates for each transaction.

## Foreign currency expenses

If you pay for business expenses in a foreign currency, the same logic applies in reverse. Convert costs to pounds sterling at the rate on the date you paid, or using HMRC's annual average rate, and claim the pound equivalent as a business expense.

Examples:
- Software subscription billed in USD
- Overseas travel for client meetings
- Fees paid to a foreign subcontractor

Keep your receipts and records of the conversion method used. HMRC may ask.

## Double taxation: how to avoid paying twice

The UK has double taxation agreements (DTAs) with over 100 countries, including the US, most of Europe, Australia, Canada, and many others. These agreements determine which country has the right to tax income — and ensure you don't pay full tax in both places.

In practice, most sole traders with foreign clients won't pay tax at source in the client's country. Most business service income flows back to the UK tax-free at source, and you pay UK tax as normal.

But if you do pay foreign tax on income — because a foreign client withheld it, or because you filed a return in another country — you can claim **Foreign Tax Credit Relief** on your UK Self Assessment return.

How it works:
1. Calculate your UK tax on the foreign income
2. Calculate the foreign tax you've already paid on the same income
3. Claim a credit for the lower of the two figures
4. Your UK tax bill is reduced accordingly

<InfoCallout type="info" title="You claim the relief — it doesn't happen automatically">
Double taxation relief does not apply automatically. You need to claim it on your Self Assessment return (the SA106 Foreign pages). If you've paid tax abroad and don't claim the credit, you'll pay full UK tax on top — effectively taxed twice. Don't leave this unclaimed.
</InfoCallout>

## Which Self Assessment forms cover foreign income

Different types of overseas income land on different parts of your tax return:

| Type of income | Where it goes |
|---|---|
| Foreign self-employment income (you worked for overseas clients as a sole trader) | SA103 Self-employment pages, with foreign income declared there |
| Foreign employment income (you were employed by an overseas employer) | SA102 Employment pages + SA106 Foreign pages |
| Foreign property income (you rent out property abroad) | SA106 Foreign pages |
| Foreign dividends or savings interest | SA106 Foreign pages |

Most sole traders with overseas clients only need the SA103 (Self-employment) and potentially SA106 if they're claiming foreign tax credit relief. But get this right — using the wrong section can cause HMRC to misallocate your income.

## MTD and foreign income: what to know

Under Making Tax Digital for Income Tax, sole traders with qualifying income above the relevant threshold must submit quarterly digital updates.

Foreign self-employment income is included in your qualifying income. So if you earn £20,000 from UK clients and £15,000 from US clients, your qualifying income is £35,000 — well above the April 2026 threshold of £50,000 in Year 1, or £30,000 in Year 2.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="A common MTD mistake with overseas income">
Some sole traders forget to include overseas client income in their quarterly MTD updates — they only submit UK client income and add the foreign income at year-end. That's non-compliant. Every transaction, regardless of the client's location, needs to be in your digital records and reflected in your quarterly submissions.
</InfoCallout>

What MTD-compatible software handles:
- Recording transactions in foreign currency and converting them
- Categorising income from overseas clients as self-employment income
- Including foreign income in quarterly updates automatically

If you use a spreadsheet bridging approach for MTD, you'll need to manually convert and enter foreign income figures. Software with bank feeds and multicurrency support handles this more reliably.

## Non-domicile rules: a brief note

UK domicile is a different concept from UK residency. Your domicile is broadly where you consider your permanent home — and it's a status that doesn't change quickly or easily.

If you are UK resident but not UK domiciled, special rules may apply to how your foreign income is taxed — specifically the "remittance basis", which previously meant you only paid UK tax on foreign income if you brought it into the UK. Those rules changed significantly from April 2025.

Non-domicile situations are complex. If you're in this position, or uncertain about your domicile status, get advice from a specialist tax adviser. This is not an area to navigate from a blog post.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Non-domicile rules changed in April 2025">
The UK non-domicile regime was substantially reformed in April 2025. If you previously relied on the remittance basis, your position may have changed. Get specialist advice if this applies to you.
</InfoCallout>

## Practical checklist for sole traders with overseas income

- [ ] Record every foreign invoice in pounds sterling at the time of receipt
- [ ] Choose your exchange rate method (actual or HMRC average) and apply it consistently
- [ ] Check whether any foreign tax was withheld — if so, claim the credit on SA106
- [ ] Include overseas income in your quarterly MTD updates (not just at year-end)
- [ ] Use software that handles multicurrency if you invoice regularly in foreign currencies
- [ ] If your domicile or residency is non-standard, get specialist advice

- UK tax residents pay UK income tax on worldwide income — client location is irrelevant
