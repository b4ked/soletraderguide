## What this guide covers

Invoicing sounds simple. In practice, sole traders make compliance mistakes, chase payments badly, and miss statutory rights they didn't know they had.

This guide covers what HMRC requires on every invoice, how numbering works, your legal rights when clients don't pay, VAT invoice rules, and which software handles invoicing well for sole traders.

---

## What HMRC legally requires on an invoice

Every invoice you issue must contain the following:

| Invoice element | Required? | Notes |
|----------------|-----------|-------|
| Your full name (or trading name) | Yes | Must match your registered name |
| Your business address | Yes | Can be home address |
| Invoice number | Yes | Must be sequential and unique |
| Invoice date | Yes | Date the invoice was issued |
| Description of service or goods | Yes | Must be clear enough to identify the supply |
| Amount due | Yes | Show the total clearly |
| VAT number | Only if VAT registered | See VAT section below |
| VAT amount shown separately | Only if VAT registered | Required on VAT invoices |

This is the legal minimum. Adding payment terms, bank details, and a due date is not legally required — but it is essential in practice.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Missing invoice numbers are a compliance risk">
HMRC expects sequential, unique invoice numbers as part of your business records. If you are ever subject to a compliance check, inconsistent or missing invoice numbers will raise questions. Use software or a consistent manual system — and never reuse a number.
</InfoCallout>

## Invoice numbering: the rules

Invoice numbering must be:

- **Sequential** — each invoice number must be higher than the last
- **Unique** — no two invoices can share the same number
- **Consistent** — the same format throughout (e.g. INV-001, INV-002 — not switching between formats)

You can start at any number (INV-001 or INV-100 or just 1). What you cannot do:

- Reuse a number from a cancelled invoice
- Skip large blocks of numbers without reason
- Reset your sequence without documenting why

If you make a mistake on an invoice, issue a **credit note** against the original and raise a fresh invoice with a new number. Do not just delete or overwrite the original.

## Payment terms: what works in practice

Standard UK payment terms for sole traders:

- **30 days** — standard for business-to-business work
- **14 days** — common for freelancers and project-based work
- **7 days** — used where fast payment is expected (e.g. small one-off jobs)

Always state your payment terms clearly on every invoice. If you do not state terms, the Late Payment of Commercial Debts Act implies 30 days from invoice date for business-to-business transactions.

### Stating your terms

Include this on every invoice:

> "Payment due within [X] days of invoice date."

Add your bank details (sort code and account number) on the invoice so clients can pay immediately without asking.

## Late payment: your statutory rights

If a client does not pay on time, you have legal rights under the **Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998**.

### What you can charge

- **Statutory interest**: 8% above the Bank of England base rate (calculated daily on the overdue amount)
- **Debt recovery costs**: a fixed compensation charge on top of the overdue amount

| Amount owed | Fixed compensation |
|------------|-------------------|
| Up to £999.99 | £40 |
| £1,000 to £9,999.99 | £70 |
| £10,000 or more | £100 |

You do not need to include these rights in your contract — they apply automatically by law for business-to-business transactions.

<InfoCallout type="tip" title="Late payment interest is a statutory right — you don't need to ask permission">
You are legally entitled to charge statutory interest on overdue business invoices. You do not need it written into your contract. Many sole traders don't know this. Mention it politely in your late payment chase — it changes the dynamic.
</InfoCallout>

### How to chase invoices professionally

Chasing payments is uncomfortable, but the key is to be systematic rather than aggressive:

1. **Day 1 after due date** — friendly reminder email: "Just checking in — invoice INV-047 was due yesterday. Can you confirm payment is on its way?"
2. **Day 7** — firm follow-up: reference the invoice number, amount, and original due date
3. **Day 14** — formal letter: state that statutory interest is now accruing, give a final payment deadline of 7 days
4. **Day 21+** — consider a formal letter before action, or small claims court for amounts under £10,000

Keep a paper trail. Every chase should be in writing (email is fine).

## VAT invoices: additional requirements

If you are VAT registered, you must issue a **VAT invoice** — which has additional mandatory fields on top of the standard requirements:

| Additional VAT invoice field | Notes |
|-----------------------------|-------|
| Your VAT registration number | Required on every VAT invoice |
| Tax point (time of supply) | Usually the invoice date, unless a deposit was paid |
| VAT rate applied | e.g. 20% standard rate |
| VAT amount shown separately | Must be clearly itemised |
| Net amount (ex VAT) | Shown separately from VAT |
| Gross amount (inc VAT) | Total amount the customer pays |

If you are not VAT registered, **never add VAT to an invoice**. Charging VAT when you are not registered is a criminal offence.

## Making Tax Digital and your invoices

Under MTD for Income Tax, your invoices become part of your **digital records**. HMRC requires that sole traders keep digital records of all income and expenses — and invoices are your primary income record.

This means:

- Paper invoices need to be digitised (photographed or scanned)
- Storing invoices in a spreadsheet or accounting software satisfies the digital record requirement
- You must be able to produce invoices during a compliance check

<InfoCallout type="deadline" title="MTD record-keeping starts from your qualifying date">
If your qualifying income exceeds £50,000, MTD applies from April 2026. From that date, your invoices must be kept as digital records. If you are still issuing paper invoices, the time to change your system is now — not in April.
</InfoCallout>

**FreeAgent** is purpose-built for freelancers and sole traders. It handles invoicing, chasing, expense recording, and Self Assessment in one place. Many sole traders get it free through NatWest or Royal Bank of Scotland business accounts.

## Practical checklist: what to include on every invoice

- [ ] Your full name or trading name
- [ ] Your business address
- [ ] Invoice number (sequential, unique)
- [ ] Invoice date
- [ ] Clear description of the service or goods supplied
- [ ] Amount due (net, VAT if applicable, gross)
- [ ] Your VAT number (VAT registered only)
- [ ] Payment due date
- [ ] Your bank details (sort code, account number)
- [ ] Payment terms stated clearly
