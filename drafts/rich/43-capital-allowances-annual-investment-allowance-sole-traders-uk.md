## Why equipment purchases need their own tax rules

When you buy a box of pens for your business, you write it off as an expense. Simple.

When you buy a £3,000 laptop, it's different. That laptop doesn't just benefit your business this year — it'll still be going in three or four years. HMRC's capital allowances system exists to give you tax relief on these longer-lasting business purchases.

The good news for sole traders: the Annual Investment Allowance means that for most equipment purchases, you can write off 100% of the cost in year one anyway. The complexity is smaller than it looks.

---

## What are capital allowances?

Capital allowances are HMRC's system for giving you tax relief on capital expenditure — money spent on assets you'll use in your business over more than one year.

Instead of deducting these costs as regular expenses, you claim capital allowances, which reduce your taxable profit.

The main types are:

- **Annual Investment Allowance (AIA):** 100% relief in year one, up to £1 million
- **Writing Down Allowance (WDA):** Percentage relief each year on assets that don't qualify for AIA (mainly cars)
- **First Year Allowances (FYA):** 100% relief in year one for specific assets (electric vehicles, energy-efficient equipment)

For most sole traders, AIA is the one that matters.

---

## The Annual Investment Allowance explained

The AIA lets you deduct the full cost of qualifying plant and machinery from your profits in the year you buy it.

Current limit: **£1 million per year.**

In practice, almost no sole trader spends anywhere near £1 million on equipment in a single year. For the vast majority of self-employed people, the AIA means you get full tax relief on every qualifying business purchase, in the year you make it.

---

## What qualifies for AIA

| Qualifies for AIA | Does NOT qualify for AIA |
|---|---|
| Computers and laptops | Land |
| Tools and machinery | Buildings and structures |
| Office furniture and desks | Cars (separate rules apply) |
| Vans and commercial vehicles | Assets used partly for non-business purposes (proportional adjustment needed) |
| Specialist trade equipment | Leased assets |
| Cameras, audio equipment | Assets bought from a connected party in some circumstances |
| Storage equipment | |
| Workshop machinery | |

Key point: the asset must be used for your business. If you use a laptop 80% for work and 20% personally, you can only claim 80% of its cost.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Electric vehicles get 100% First Year Allowance">
If you buy a new or unused electric car for your business, you can claim a 100% First Year Allowance — meaning the full cost is deductible in year one, just like the AIA but through a different mechanism. This applies specifically to electric cars with zero CO2 emissions. For other cars, you're limited to the writing down allowance.
</InfoCallout>

---

## Cars: the exception to everything

Cars are excluded from AIA and treated differently. HMRC's reasoning: cars are regularly used privately, so they get a more conservative approach.

**Writing Down Allowance rates for cars (2025/26):**

| Car type | CO2 emissions | Annual WDA rate |
|---|---|---|
| Low-emission cars | 50g/km or less | 18% per year |
| Higher-emission cars | Over 50g/km | 6% per year |
| New/unused electric cars | 0g/km | 100% First Year Allowance |

So if you buy a petrol car for £20,000 with CO2 above 50g/km, you'd claim 6% of its value each year:
- Year 1: £1,200 deduction (6% of £20,000)
- Year 2: £1,128 deduction (6% of £18,800)
- And so on, until the value is essentially zero

For a van, you claim AIA in full in year one — much better.

## Worked example: Laptop and van

Jake is a self-employed joiner. In 2025/26 he buys:
- A laptop for £2,000
- A new diesel van for £22,000

**The laptop:**
Qualifies for AIA. Jake claims £2,000 in full in 2025/26. Done.

**The van:**
A van is a commercial vehicle, not a car. It qualifies for AIA. Jake claims £22,000 in full in 2025/26.

Total AIA claimed: **£24,000**

Jake's trading profit before these deductions was £35,000. After capital allowances, his taxable profit is £11,000. That's a significant tax saving.

If Jake had bought a car instead of a van, he'd only get 18% or 6% in year one, not the full amount.

## The cash basis trap: a different set of rules

Here's something that catches many sole traders off guard.

If you use **cash basis accounting** (the simplified method HMRC allows for most sole traders), capital allowances mostly don't apply to you in the traditional sense.

Under cash basis:
- Most equipment you buy for your business is treated as an **allowable expense** directly, in the year you pay for it
- You don't go through the capital allowances system at all for these items
- The deduction is the same — 100% of cost in year one — but the mechanism is simpler

The practical effect is often identical, but you need to know which system you're using and apply the right rules.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Cash basis and capital allowances work differently">
If you use cash basis accounting, you claim most equipment costs as allowable expenses — not as capital allowances. Cars are the main exception: even under cash basis, you still use capital allowances rules for vehicles. If you're unsure which basis you use, check with your accountant or review your Self Assessment return.
</InfoCallout>

**Under cash basis, capital allowances still apply to:**
- Cars (always use capital allowances, regardless of accounting basis)
- Some specific asset types excluded from the cash basis expense rules

**Under accruals accounting (traditional accounting):**
- Use the full capital allowances system, including AIA, WDA, and FYA

## Writing down allowances: when AIA doesn't cover everything

In a small number of cases, you might have assets that don't qualify for AIA (cars being the main one) or you might have bought an asset in a previous year and are continuing to claim on its remaining value.

Writing down allowances let you claim a percentage of the asset's remaining value each year.

**Main rate pool:** 18% per year (most plant and machinery, low-emission cars)
**Special rate pool:** 6% per year (higher-emission cars, some long-life assets)

These are declining balance calculations — you claim a percentage of the remaining value each year, not the original cost.

---

## What the super-deduction was (and why it doesn't apply)

Between April 2021 and March 2023, incorporated companies (limited companies, not sole traders) could claim a temporary 130% super-deduction on qualifying plant and machinery.

This meant a company spending £100,000 on equipment could deduct £130,000 from profits.

**It ended in April 2023 and only ever applied to companies — never to sole traders.** If you see references to it online, ignore them. The current system for sole traders is AIA at 100% up to £1 million.

## Practical checklist: claiming capital allowances as a sole trader

- [ ] Identify all assets purchased this year for business use
- [ ] Confirm each asset qualifies (not land, buildings, or non-business items)
- [ ] Note any private use proportion and adjust your claim accordingly
- [ ] Check whether you're on cash basis or accruals — this determines your route
- [ ] For vehicles, identify whether it's a car or a commercial vehicle
- [ ] For electric cars: claim 100% FYA
- [ ] For other cars: calculate WDA at 18% or 6% based on CO2
- [ ] Record all purchases with receipts and dates
- [ ] Claim in your Self Assessment return under the capital allowances section

---

## Summary

Capital allowances exist so you can get tax relief on equipment and assets you buy for your business. For most sole traders, the Annual Investment Allowance means you write off 100% of qualifying costs in year one — up to £1 million — with no complicated spreading over multiple years.

Cars are the exception: they use writing down allowances at 18% or 6% per year, except electric cars which get 100% in year one.

If you're on cash basis, most equipment costs are claimed as expenses rather than capital allowances — but the result is usually the same.

Keep receipts, track business use proportions, and claim everything you're entitled to.
