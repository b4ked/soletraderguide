## The short version

MTD requires your business records to be kept digitally. But "digital" has a specific meaning in HMRC's rules — and it's not just "not on paper".

The things that must be digital: the transaction data for every business income and expense — date, amount, category — recorded individually in software or a connected spreadsheet.

The things that don't need to be digital: supporting documents like paper receipts and invoices. You can keep those as paper. HMRC cares about the data, not the documents.

And the part that catches most people out: if you use two different systems, they must be digitally linked. No manual copying of totals between them.

---

## What HMRC means by "digital records"

HMRC's MTD legislation requires that for each transaction, you record:

- The date of the transaction
- The amount (gross amount for income; amount paid for expenses)
- The category (what type of income or expense it is)

That's the minimum. Each transaction individually. Not weekly totals. Not monthly summaries. Each one.

This data must be held in a digital format — software, a spreadsheet, or a connected combination of both — from which your quarterly updates can be generated.

## What you must record digitally

| Record type | Must be digital? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Each business income transaction | Yes | Date, amount, and category for every sale |
| Each business expense transaction | Yes | Date, amount, and category for every cost |
| VAT information (if VAT registered) | Yes | Already required under MTD for VAT |
| Employee payments | Yes (via RTI if you have staff) | Handled through payroll separately |
| Supporting documents (receipts, invoices) | No | Paper is fine — it's the transaction data that must be digital |
| Bank statements | No (but bank feeds make compliance easier) | Reconciling digitally is best practice, not a legal requirement |

---

## What does NOT need to be digital

This is the part most people get wrong — in both directions.

**You do not need to scan your paper receipts.** HMRC does not require supporting documents to be held digitally. A paper receipt in a folder is fine as long as the transaction it relates to is recorded digitally in your system.

**You do not need to digitise old paper records.** The digital record-keeping requirement applies from your MTD start date. Prior records stay as they are.

**You do not need bank statements to be digital.** Bank feeds are a useful tool — they save hours of data entry — but they're not a legal requirement under MTD. You can enter transactions manually as long as the data is recorded correctly.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Paper receipts are fine — it's the transaction data that must be digital">
HMRC's digital record-keeping requirement applies to transaction data, not supporting documents. Keep your paper receipts if that's easier. What matters is that each transaction — date, amount, category — is in your digital records. The receipt is just the evidence behind it.
</InfoCallout>

## The digital link rule: the part that catches people out

If you use more than one system — for example, a spreadsheet to track expenses and separate software for invoicing — there must be a **digital link** between them.

A digital link means the data flows between systems without anyone manually retyping it.

**Compliant digital links include:**
- Automated data feeds or API connections between two pieces of software
- Importing a CSV from one system into another
- Copy-pasting data within the same software package (within a single program is allowed)
- Using bridging software that reads your spreadsheet and submits to HMRC

**Not a digital link:**
- Writing a monthly total from your spreadsheet and retyping it into software
- Printing a report from one system and typing the figures into another
- Emailing yourself numbers and manually entering them elsewhere

The digital link rule means your records must form an unbroken digital chain from transaction to quarterly submission.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="The digital link rule catches out multi-system setups">
If you track some things in a spreadsheet and other things in software, and you manually copy figures between them, that's non-compliant. The data must flow digitally — with no human retyping in the middle. Bridging software exists specifically to solve this for spreadsheet users.
</InfoCallout>

## Compliant vs non-compliant setups

| Setup | Compliant? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Bank feed directly into MTD software | Yes | Fully digital, no manual entry required |
| Photo of receipt via app (Dext, Hubdoc) → software | Yes | Digital link from capture to records |
| Digital invoice emailed → uploaded to software | Yes | Transaction enters software directly |
| Spreadsheet → bridging software → HMRC | Yes | Bridging software creates the digital link |
| Writing monthly totals from a notebook into software | No | Not digital at source; summarised totals not allowed |
| Printing bank statements and retyping figures | No | Manual transfer breaks the digital link |
| Spreadsheet totals manually copied into software | No | No digital link between the two systems |
| Using two pieces of software with no integration | No (without a digital link) | Data must flow digitally between them |

## Receipt capture apps: worth knowing about

Two tools designed specifically for this problem:

**Dext (formerly Receipt Bank):** Photograph a receipt with your phone. Dext reads the data, categorises it, and pushes the transaction into your accounting software automatically. Integrates with most major MTD-compatible software including Sage, Xero, QuickBooks, and FreeAgent.

**Hubdoc:** Similar functionality — capture receipts and bills, extract the data, push to your accounting software. Included free with Xero subscriptions.

These tools solve two problems at once: they digitise your evidence trail (for your own records) and they create a compliant digital link between the physical receipt and your accounting system.

Neither is required for MTD compliance — but if you deal with lots of paper receipts, they save significant time.

## How dedicated MTD software handles this

When you use MTD-compatible accounting software (Sage, Xero, QuickBooks, FreeAgent, and others), the digital record-keeping requirements are largely handled for you:

- Transactions from your bank come in via bank feed
- Receipts can be attached via mobile app
- Each transaction is recorded individually with date, amount, and category
- Quarterly submissions pull directly from your records with one click

The software is designed around MTD's requirements. If you enter your transactions properly, you're compliant — without needing to think about digital links, data formats, or submission processes.

## If you're using a spreadsheet

Spreadsheets can be MTD compliant — but only with bridging software.

The spreadsheet records your transactions. The bridging software reads those records and submits them to HMRC in the correct format. The two systems are digitally linked through the bridging process.

What you cannot do: record transactions in a spreadsheet and then manually retype totals into HMRC's portal or any other system. That breaks the digital link.

If you want to stay on a spreadsheet for MTD, the setup looks like this:

1. Record each transaction in your spreadsheet (date, amount, category — each one individually)
2. Connect your spreadsheet to bridging software (providers include 123 Sheets, GoSimpleTax, and others)
3. The bridging software reads your records and submits your quarterly update to HMRC

This works. It's more manual than dedicated software, but it's compliant — and it keeps your costs low if you're already comfortable with spreadsheets.

## Quick summary

- MTD requires each transaction to be recorded digitally with date, amount, and category
- Supporting documents (paper receipts) do not need to be digitised
- Each transaction must be individual — no monthly totals or summaries
- If you use two systems, they must be digitally linked — no manual retyping between them
- Bank feeds, receipt apps, and CSV imports are all compliant digital links
- Writing totals from one place and typing them into another is not
- Spreadsheets work for MTD if used with bridging software
- Dedicated MTD software handles the requirements automatically
