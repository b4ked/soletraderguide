## The rule most new sole traders miss

Most people registering as self-employed focus on expenses from the day they officially start trading. But HMRC has a rule that lets you look further back — up to 7 years before your business began.

If you spent money getting ready to trade — buying equipment, taking a specialist course, hiring a professional to set things up — and that spending would have been an allowable expense had your business already been running, you can claim it.

This single rule is worth knowing about before you file your first Self Assessment return.

---

## The HMRC rule explained

The relevant legislation is ITTOIA 2005, section 57. In plain English, it says:

> You can deduct pre-trading expenditure incurred within 7 years before your trade begins, if it would have been allowable as a deduction had it been incurred after the trade started.

The test is: **would this expense have been allowable if I had already been trading?**

If yes — and it was incurred within 7 years before your start date — it is claimable.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="The 7-year look-back period">
HMRC's 7-year pre-trade expenses window is generous and underused. Many new sole traders don't claim what they're entitled to because they didn't know costs before their start date could count. Your first Self Assessment return is the place to include them.
</InfoCallout>

## What you can claim

### Equipment and tools

If you purchased equipment before starting your business that you now use in it, you can claim the cost.

Examples:
- A laptop or desktop computer
- Professional tools, instruments, or specialist equipment
- Camera gear for a photography business
- Drawing tablet for a design business

The equipment must be in active business use after you start trading. If you bought it purely personally and the business is incidental, it does not qualify.

### Software and subscriptions

Software licences and professional subscriptions that are specific to your trade can be claimed.

Examples:
- Adobe Creative Cloud purchased before a design business launched
- Accounting software set up before the first invoice was sent
- Specialist industry software required for your work

### Training and courses

Training costs are claimable if the training was **directly and specifically** related to the trade you are now carrying on.

What qualifies:
- A specialist technical course required for your specific trade
- Professional certification needed to work in your field

What does not qualify:
- General business or management training not specific to your trade
- A broad educational qualification that happens to be related to your field
- Any training that would have been useful regardless of your business

This area has a high bar. The training must exist because of the specific trade you are operating, not because it was generally useful.

### Professional fees

Fees paid to accountants, solicitors, or other professionals to help you set up your business.

Examples:
- Accountant fees for initial setup advice
- Legal fees for contract templates
- Business registration costs

### Website and marketing costs

Website setup costs, domain registration, and early marketing spend can qualify — provided they relate specifically to the business that then commenced trading.

## What you cannot claim

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Keep all receipts from before you officially started">
Even if you are not sure whether a pre-trade expense qualifies, keep the receipt. It is far better to have documentation and decide later than to lose the evidence and be unable to make the claim at all.
</InfoCallout>

The following do not qualify as pre-trade expenses:

- **Personal expenses** that existed before you decided to start the business (e.g. a home broadband contract that predates any business thinking)
- **General education** that is not specifically connected to your trade
- **Capital expenditure** handled separately through capital allowances (different process — speak to an accountant if significant sums are involved)
- **Expenses that would not have been allowable anyway** — the pre-trade rule mirrors the main trading expense rules, it does not expand them

## Worked example: Sara's pre-trade expenses

Sara is a freelance graphic designer. She registered as self-employed on 1 January 2026.

In the 12 months before that date, Sara spent:

| Expense | Amount | Claimable? |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Adobe Creative Cloud (12 months) | £660 | Yes |
| Drawing tablet (Wacom) | £450 | Yes |
| Advanced typography course | £380 | Yes — specialist to her trade |
| MacBook Pro | £1,200 | Yes (80% business use = £960) |
| General productivity app subscription | £110 | Borderline — if wholly for business, yes |
| Home broadband (personal contract) | £480 | No — pre-existing personal expense |

**Total claimable pre-trade expenses: approximately £2,560**

Sara includes these on her first Self Assessment return in the relevant expense boxes. HMRC treats them as if they were incurred on 1 January 2026.

At a 20% tax rate, the tax saving is around **£512**.

## How to claim on your Self Assessment return

The process is straightforward:

1. Add up all qualifying pre-trade expenses, categorised by type (equipment, subscriptions, professional fees, etc.)
2. On your Self Assessment return, enter them in the **same expense boxes** you use for ongoing trading expenses
3. HMRC treats them as if incurred on the first day of trading — you do not need to flag them separately
4. Keep all receipts, invoices, and bank statements as evidence

If you are using accounting software that pre-dates your trading start date, you may be able to record these expenses as if they occurred on day one. If you are unsure how to enter them, an accountant can do this correctly on your first return.

<InfoCallout type="tip" title="Your first Self Assessment return is the time to get this right">
Pre-trade expenses can only be claimed on your first Self Assessment return for the tax year in which you started trading. If you miss them, it is possible to amend a return but more complicated. Do a full review of your pre-start spending before you file.
</InfoCallout>
