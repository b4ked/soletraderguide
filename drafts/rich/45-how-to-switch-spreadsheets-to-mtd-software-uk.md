## The problem with staying on spreadsheets

You've probably been managing your income and expenses in Excel or Google Sheets for years. It works. You know where everything is. It doesn't cost you anything.

The issue is that HMRC's Making Tax Digital for Income Tax requires digital records to be kept in HMRC-recognised software — and your spreadsheet almost certainly doesn't qualify on its own.

From April 2026 (income over £50k) and April 2027 (income over £30k), you'll need to submit quarterly income and expense updates directly to HMRC. A standard spreadsheet cannot do that.

Your options are:
1. Switch fully to MTD-compatible accounting software
2. Use bridging software that sits between your spreadsheet and HMRC

For most sole traders, option one is the better long-term call. This guide covers how to do it.

---

## Bridging software: the spreadsheet compromise

Before we get into the full migration, it's worth understanding bridging software — because some people will genuinely prefer it.

Bridging tools connect your spreadsheet to HMRC's MTD system. You maintain your records in Excel or Google Sheets as usual, and the bridging software reads from it and submits your quarterly updates to HMRC.

**Pros:**
- Keep your existing spreadsheet format
- No migration needed
- Often cheaper

**Cons:**
- Still requires your spreadsheet to be structured in a specific way
- Adds a submission step each quarter
- No bank feeds, no automation
- Less visibility and less useful as a business management tool
- Another piece of software to manage on top of your spreadsheet

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Bridging software is valid but limited">
Bridging software is HMRC-compliant, but it doesn't do your bookkeeping for you. If your spreadsheet is messy or inconsistent, bridging software won't fix that — it'll just submit the mess to HMRC faster. For sole traders who want a genuinely easier life, full MTD software is worth the switch.
</InfoCallout>

The rest of this guide assumes you're making a full switch. If you're set on bridging software, the early steps (assess, reconcile, choose software) still apply.

## Step 1: Assess what you're working with

Before you touch anything, understand the state of your current records.

Go through your spreadsheet and check:

- [ ] Is your income recording complete and accurate for the current tax year?
- [ ] Are all expenses categorised consistently?
- [ ] Have you reconciled against your bank statements recently?
- [ ] Do you know your current bank balance and how it compares to your spreadsheet?
- [ ] Do you have unpaid invoices outstanding? (debtors)
- [ ] Do you have bills you've received but not yet paid? (creditors)
- [ ] If you're on accruals basis: do you have a list of business assets?

The cleaner your spreadsheet is before you start, the simpler the migration will be.

## Step 2: Choose your MTD software

All of the major options are HMRC-recognised and will handle quarterly MTD submissions. The differences are in how easy they are to set up, import your data, and use day-to-day.

**Migration ease comparison:**

| Software | Import tools | Onboarding support | Setup time | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FreeAgent | Good CSV import | Excellent guided setup | 2–4 hours | Freelancers, simpler businesses |
| Sage | Good import tools | Strong support, CIS module | 3–5 hours | Tradespeople, CIS, more complex |
| QuickBooks | Strong import | Good guided setup | 3–5 hours | General use, VAT-registered |
| Xero | Strong import | Good, less hand-holding | 4–6 hours | Growing businesses |

If your priority is getting set up fast with minimal friction, FreeAgent is the easiest for most sole traders. If you're in construction or need CIS support, Sage is the standout choice.

## Step 3: Reconcile your spreadsheet before you migrate

Do not skip this step.

Before you migrate anything, bring your spreadsheet fully up to date:

1. Enter every income and expense transaction up to today (or to the end of your last full month)
2. Reconcile your spreadsheet balance against your actual bank statement
3. Resolve any discrepancies — find missing transactions, correct errors
4. Note your reconciled bank balance and date

This gives you a clean anchor point for your migration. You'll use the reconciled balance as your opening balance in the new software.

---

## Step 4: Identify what to migrate

Good news: you don't need to import years of history.

**What to bring across:**

| Item | Migrate it? | How |
|---|---|---|
| Opening bank balance (reconciled) | Yes | Enter manually as opening balance |
| Unpaid customer invoices | Yes | Enter as outstanding receivables |
| Unpaid supplier bills | Yes | Enter as outstanding payables |
| Business asset list (accruals basis) | Yes | Enter in fixed assets register |
| Current year income transactions | Optional | Import via CSV if available |
| Current year expense transactions | Optional | Import via CSV if available |
| Previous years' records | No | Keep in spreadsheet as archive |

The minimum viable migration: enter your opening bank balance and any unpaid invoices or bills. Connect your bank feed. Go forward from there.

---

## Step 5: Set up your new software

Create your account, then work through the setup:

1. Enter your business details (name, address, UTR if prompted)
2. Set your accounting basis (cash or accruals) — match what you've been using
3. Set your accounting period start date
4. Enter your opening bank balance (your reconciled figure from Step 3)
5. Enter any outstanding invoices owed to you
6. Enter any bills outstanding
7. Enter your asset list if applicable

Then connect your bank feed. This is the single biggest time-saver — once connected, transactions import automatically and you just categorise them rather than manually entering everything.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Set up before your mandatory start date — not on it">
Going live with new software takes time to get right. Bank feeds take a day or two to activate. Opening balance entries need checking. Running in parallel for a month before you fully switch is the safest approach. If your mandatory MTD start date is April 2026, aim to be live and running by January or February 2026.
</InfoCallout>

---

## Step 6: Import historical current-year data (optional but useful)

If you want more complete records in your new software — not just a clean start from today — you can import your current-year transactions via CSV.

Most accounting software allows CSV imports of transactions. Your bank will also let you export a CSV of your transaction history.

Steps:
1. Export transactions from your bank for the current tax year
2. Clean the CSV (remove personal transactions, ensure consistent formatting)
3. Import into your software using its import tool
4. Categorise each transaction

This can be time-consuming. For most sole traders, it's optional — the opening balance approach in Step 4 is sufficient.

---

## Step 7: Run in parallel for one month

Before you abandon your spreadsheet entirely, run both in parallel for one full month:

- Record income and expenses in both your old spreadsheet and your new software
- At month end, compare totals
- Investigate any discrepancies and resolve them

This one month of parallel running catches data entry errors, miscategorised transactions, and opening balance mistakes before they compound. It's the most important step most people skip.

---

## Step 8: Connect to HMRC and go live

Once you're confident your records are accurate in your new software:

1. Complete the MTD ITSA sign-up process via Government Gateway (see our separate guide)
2. Authorise your software to connect to HMRC
3. Retire your spreadsheet (but keep it as an archive — don't delete it)
4. Submit your quarterly updates on schedule going forward

From this point, your software is your record of truth.

---

## What not to do

**Don't try to import everything.** Years of history creates noise without value. Focus on your current position.

**Don't migrate mid-quarter without a plan.** If you switch halfway through a quarter, be clear about which system covers which dates, and don't double-count.

**Don't assume your spreadsheet categories map to software categories.** Your "general expenses" bucket may need splitting into several HMRC categories. Review each expense type and map it correctly.

**Don't skip the parallel month.** The month of parallel running is how you catch problems cheaply. Skipping it means finding problems at your first quarterly deadline instead.

| Week | Action |
|---|---|
| Week 1 | Assess and reconcile your spreadsheet. Choose software. |
| Week 2 | Set up account, enter opening balances, connect bank feed. |
| Week 3–6 | Run in parallel. Catch discrepancies. |

Total time: roughly six to eight weeks from decision to fully live. Start this process well before your mandatory compliance date.
