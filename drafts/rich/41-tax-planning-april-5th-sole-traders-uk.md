## April 5 is the deadline that matters

The UK tax year closes at midnight on 5 April. Once it passes, your options for that year are gone.

Most sole traders focus on January (Self Assessment deadline) and miss the quieter but often more valuable April 5 window. This is where proactive tax planning happens — pension contributions, year-end purchases, allowance use-up.

Here are 8 specific actions worth reviewing before the tax year closes.

<InfoCallout type="deadline" title="Everything on this list must be done before 5 April">
No exceptions. Pension contributions, ISA top-ups, payments on account applications — all must be in before midnight on 5 April to count for the 2025/26 tax year.
</InfoCallout>

---

## The 8 actions

### 1. Maximise pension contributions

Pension contributions reduce your taxable profit. Every £1 you put into a pension is a £1 that HMRC doesn't tax (up to your annual allowance).

For 2025/26:
- Annual pension allowance: £60,000 (or 100% of your earnings, whichever is lower)
- Basic rate relief: 20% automatically added by your pension provider
- Higher rate relief: claim the additional 20-25% through your tax return

**Worked example:** A sole trader with a profit of £55,000 pays 40% on £4,730 (the slice above £50,270). A £4,730 pension contribution before 5 April eliminates that higher-rate exposure — saving approximately £946.

If you haven't used your full allowance in the past three years, you may be able to carry forward unused allowance from earlier years and contribute more than £60,000 this year. Speak to an IFA if you're in this position.

---

### 2. Review year-end purchases

If you were planning to buy equipment, software, or tools for your business, buying before 5 April means the cost falls into this tax year — reducing this year's taxable profit.

The Annual Investment Allowance (AIA) lets you deduct the full cost of qualifying plant and machinery in the year of purchase, up to £1 million. For most sole traders, the full cost of any equipment will be deductible in year one.

**Worth reviewing:** a new laptop, camera, tools, office furniture, specialist equipment. If you need it and you're going to buy it anyway — buy it before 5 April.

| Action | Who it applies to | Potential saving |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| Pension contributions | Any sole trader with profit over personal allowance | 20–45% of amount contributed |
| Year-end equipment purchases | Sole traders who need equipment | 20–45% tax relief on cost |
| Review payments on account | Anyone whose profit has dropped year-on-year | Cash flow relief |
| Trading allowance check | Part-time or side-income sole traders | Up to £1,000 tax-free |
| Gift Aid donations | Basic rate taxpayers wanting to extend their band | Marginal tax saving |
| Marriage allowance | Couples where one earns below personal allowance | Up to £252/year |
| Higher rate planning | Sole traders near the £50,270 threshold | Avoid 40% on marginal income |
| ISA top-up | Anyone with savings | Tax-free growth, use-it-or-lose-it |

### 3. Check your trading allowance

If your self-employment income is modest — side income, casual work, a small hobby business — you may qualify for the £1,000 trading allowance.

If your gross trading income is £1,000 or less, you don't need to report it or pay tax on it. If it's above £1,000, you can either:
- deduct the £1,000 trading allowance (instead of actual expenses), or
- deduct your actual expenses (if they're higher)

If you have a small secondary income stream, check whether the trading allowance covers it fully. If it does, you can keep it entirely tax-free with zero record-keeping required.

---

### 4. Review your payments on account

If your profit has been lower than the previous year — due to fewer clients, illness, maternity, or any other reason — you may be able to apply to reduce your July 2026 payment on account.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Apply to reduce in advance — not after">
You must apply to reduce payments on account before the payment deadline. If you simply pay less without applying, HMRC charges interest on the shortfall. Log in to Government Gateway and use the 'Reduce payments on account' function.
</InfoCallout>

Payments on account are based on the previous year's liability. If your 2025/26 profit is materially lower than 2024/25, check whether your July payment should be reduced. This doesn't save tax — but it improves cash flow significantly.

---

### 5. Gift Aid donations

If you're a basic rate taxpayer, Gift Aid donations allow charities to reclaim 20% tax on your donation. If you're a higher rate taxpayer, you can claim the difference between basic and higher rate through your tax return.

**Higher rate example:** A £1,000 Gift Aid donation reclaims 25p in the pound for the charity (£250). You claim the additional 20% (£200) through your tax return, making the effective cost to you £800 for a £1,250 gross donation.

Donations also extend your basic rate band, which can keep you below the 40% threshold. If you're a regular donor, make sure you're claiming this on your return.

---

### 6. Marriage allowance

If you're married or in a civil partnership and one partner earns below the personal allowance (£12,570), they can transfer up to £1,260 of their unused personal allowance to the higher earner.

This reduces the higher earner's tax bill by up to £252 per year (£1,260 × 20%). It's free to apply through HMRC, can be backdated up to four years, and is often overlooked.

Not applicable if either partner pays higher or additional rate tax.

---

### 7. Higher rate planning — stay below £50,270

The higher rate threshold for 2025/26 is £50,270. Every pound above this is taxed at 40% instead of 20%.

If you're on track to breach this threshold, the most effective tool is pension contributions. They reduce your taxable profit, not just your income — so a £5,000 pension contribution when you're at £55,000 profit keeps you entirely in the basic rate band.

**Worked example:**

| Scenario | Taxable profit | Higher rate exposure | Higher rate tax owed |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| No pension contribution | £55,000 | £4,730 | £1,892 |
| £4,730 pension contribution | £50,270 | £0 | £0 |
| Net cost of pension contribution | — | — | £4,730 − £1,892 = £2,838 effective cost |

The £4,730 pension contribution costs you £2,838 in real terms once the tax saving is factored in. You still have the money — it's just in a pension rather than in your bank account.

---

### 8. ISA top-up

The annual ISA allowance is £20,000. It resets on 6 April. You cannot carry it forward.

If you have savings sitting in a taxable account earning interest (which is now taxable above the personal savings allowance), moving money into a Cash ISA or Stocks and Shares ISA shelters future growth and interest from tax.

This won't help your tax bill for 2025/26 directly — ISA contributions don't reduce your taxable profit. But it builds a tax-free savings pot for the future and removes future income from taxable accounts.

Use it or lose it. The deadline is 5 April.
