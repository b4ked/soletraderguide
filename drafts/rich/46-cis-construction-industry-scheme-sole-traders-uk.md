## What is CIS and why does it affect sole traders?

The Construction Industry Scheme (CIS) is HMRC's system for collecting tax from workers in the construction sector. Instead of waiting until the end of the year when subcontractors file their tax return, CIS requires contractors to deduct tax from payments to subcontractors at source — upfront, every time they pay.

This affects a large number of self-employed people: builders, plumbers, electricians, roofers, groundworkers, decorators, dryliners, and many others who work in the construction supply chain.

If you work in any of these trades, you are almost certainly operating within CIS — whether you know it or not.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="CIS applies even to small subcontractors">
There is no minimum earnings threshold for CIS. Even if you do occasional subcontracting work alongside other self-employment, any qualifying construction payments made to you by a contractor must go through CIS. Your contractor has no choice — they must deduct and account for CIS tax on those payments.
</InfoCallout>

## Two roles in CIS: subcontractor and contractor

Your obligations under CIS depend on which role you're in — and some sole traders operate as both simultaneously.

### If you're a subcontractor

You are paid by a contractor to do construction work.

**Your obligations:**
- Register with HMRC as a CIS subcontractor (free, done through Government Gateway)
- Provide your UTR and National Insurance number to contractors who pay you
- Receive payments with CIS deductions already made (20% if registered, 30% if not)
- Declare your gross income and CIS deductions in your Self Assessment return
- Offset CIS deductions against your annual tax bill

You do not submit CIS returns. That's the contractor's job. Your main task is keeping records of what you've been paid gross and what has been deducted — and making sure it matches the statements your contractors should be giving you (CIS statements are issued monthly by contractors).

### If you're a contractor

You take on construction projects and subcontract some or all of the work to others.

**Your obligations:**
- Register with HMRC as a CIS contractor (different registration from subcontractor)
- Verify each new subcontractor with HMRC before paying them for the first time
- Deduct the correct amount of CIS tax from each payment (20% for registered subcontractors, 30% for unregistered)
- Pay the deducted tax to HMRC
- Submit a monthly CIS return to HMRC by the 19th of each month
- Provide monthly payment and deduction statements to each subcontractor

This is significantly more administration than subcontracting. If you are contracting out work, you need proper systems in place.

## CIS deduction rates

When you're a registered CIS subcontractor, your contractor deducts **20%** from the labour element of each payment (not materials — materials are paid gross).

If you're **not** registered as a CIS subcontractor, the rate is **30%**. Registering is free and takes minutes. There is no good reason not to register.

**Example:**
Emma is a sole trader plasterer. She invoices a contractor for:
- Labour: £2,000
- Materials: £400

Total invoice: £2,400

The contractor pays:
- Materials: £400 (in full, no deduction)
- Labour: £2,000 minus 20% CIS deduction = £1,600

Emma receives **£2,000** in cash. The **£400** CIS deduction goes directly to HMRC.

Emma records £2,400 as gross income, and £400 as a CIS deduction. When she does her Self Assessment, the £400 is credited against her tax bill.

## Gross payment status: getting paid in full

If you have a good compliance record, you can apply to receive **gross payment status** — meaning contractors pay you in full with no CIS deductions.

You then manage your own tax liability through Self Assessment quarterly payments on account and your annual return.

**To qualify for gross payment status you need:**
- To be registered for Self Assessment
- A good tax payment and filing record (no significant late payments or returns)
- A UK bank account in your business or personal name
- To meet HMRC's net construction turnover threshold (currently around £30,000 per year for a sole trader)

<InfoCallout type="tip" title="Apply for gross payment status if you qualify">
Gross payment status gives you full control over your cash flow. With standard CIS, 20% of every labour payment is withheld until your tax return. That can mean waiting up to a year to recover overpaid tax. If you're eligible, apply — the process is straightforward via Government Gateway.
</InfoCallout>

Gross payment status is reviewed by HMRC annually. If your compliance record slips — late returns, unpaid tax — HMRC can revoke it.

## How CIS interacts with MTD for Income Tax

This is where things get more complex for CIS sole traders — and where getting the right software matters.

Under MTD ITSA, you'll submit quarterly updates of your income and expenses to HMRC. For CIS subcontractors, your quarterly updates must correctly reflect:

1. **Gross income:** The full amount you invoiced (before CIS deductions)
2. **CIS deductions suffered:** The amount deducted by your contractors
3. **Allowable expenses:** Your business costs as normal

If you only record the cash you actually received (after CIS deductions), your income figures will be understated and your tax position will be wrong.

Most MTD-compatible software allows you to record gross income and CIS deductions as separate fields. But not all software handles this equally well.

## Common CIS mistakes sole traders make

**1. Recording received cash instead of gross income**
When your contractor pays you £1,600 after deducting £400 CIS, your income for tax purposes is £2,000 — not £1,600. Recording only what hits your bank account understates your income.

**2. Not verifying subcontractors before paying (if you're a contractor)**
Paying a subcontractor without verifying their CIS status with HMRC first can result in you applying the wrong deduction rate — and you're liable for the shortfall.

**3. Not collecting CIS deduction statements**
Your contractors should be giving you a monthly CIS payment and deduction statement. Keep these. They are your evidence of what has been deducted, and you'll need them if your credits don't match HMRC's records.

**4. Missing monthly CIS contractor returns**
If you're a contractor, CIS returns are due by the 19th of each month for the previous tax month. Missing these attracts automatic penalties. There are no exceptions.

**5. Not claiming back CIS overpayments**
If more CIS tax has been deducted than your actual tax liability, HMRC owes you money. This only happens if you claim it via your Self Assessment return. Some sole traders don't realise this and leave money on the table.
