## The short version

Marriage allowance lets the lower earner in a married couple or civil partnership transfer £1,260 of their personal allowance to their partner. The partner gets a tax credit worth £252.

It sounds modest. But sole traders with variable income can qualify in years when profit dips — and you can backdate claims up to four years, which means a potential lump sum of over £1,000 if you've been eligible and haven't claimed.

Five minutes on GOV.UK. That's what this takes.

---

## How marriage allowance works

The personal allowance for 2025/26 is £12,570. If one spouse or civil partner earns less than that — and the other is a basic-rate taxpayer — the lower earner can transfer exactly £1,260 of their unused personal allowance to the higher earner.

The higher earner's tax bill drops by 20% of £1,260, which is £252.

That's it. No complicated forms. No accountant required.

The allowance is applied as a tax reducer — a direct reduction in the tax owed, not just a reduction in taxable income. That distinction matters: it's reliably worth £252 every year you're eligible, regardless of exactly how much tax your partner pays.

---

## Who qualifies

To be eligible for marriage allowance, all of the following must be true:

| Condition | Who it applies to |
|---|---|
| You are married or in a civil partnership | Both partners |
| One partner's income is below £12,570 | The transferring partner |
| The other partner's income is between £12,570 and £50,270 | The receiving partner |
| Neither partner is a Scottish taxpayer claiming at a rate above basic | Both partners |

**Who does not qualify:**

| Situation | Why it doesn't work |
|---|---|
| The receiving partner pays higher-rate tax (income above £50,270) | Not eligible — the relief is only for basic-rate taxpayers |
| Both partners earn above £12,570 | The transferring partner has no unused allowance to give |
| The couple is unmarried (including cohabiting) | Marriage allowance is for married couples and civil partners only |
| The lower earner is a Scottish taxpayer and the receiving partner is not | Complex rules apply — get specific advice |

## Why this is especially useful for sole traders

Employed workers have predictable income. Sole traders don't.

A good year followed by a quiet one — a lost client, a health issue, a career pivot, maternity — can mean your taxable profit drops below £12,570. In that year, you may have significant unused personal allowance sitting there doing nothing.

Marriage allowance lets you put it to use.

You don't need to be consistently low-earning. You qualify in any year where your income falls below the threshold. And if you've had several such years recently, backdating can recover a meaningful sum.

<InfoCallout type="tip" title="Variable income makes this worth reviewing annually">
Even if you weren't eligible last year, check again this year. If your sole trader profit fluctuates, your eligibility for marriage allowance can change year to year. Build it into your annual tax review.
</InfoCallout>

## Worked example

**The situation:** James is a self-employed graphic designer. In 2025/26, his profit is £8,000. His wife, Sarah, is employed and earns £35,000.

**The maths:**

- James's income: £8,000 — well below the £12,570 personal allowance
- James has £4,570 of unused personal allowance
- He can transfer £1,260 of it to Sarah
- Sarah receives a tax credit of £252 (20% of £1,260), applied against her income tax bill

**Result:** Sarah's tax bill drops by £252. James's tax bill stays at zero (he wasn't paying income tax anyway — his income is below the threshold).

**Net gain for the household: £252 — for five minutes of admin.**

## Backdating: how to claim up to £1,008 in one go

Marriage allowance can be backdated up to four tax years. You do this through a single application on GOV.UK — it takes around five minutes.

Each year you backdate is worth up to £252 (the maximum for that year's rules). So:

| Tax year | Maximum claim |
|---|---|
| 2025/26 | £252 |
| 2024/25 | £252 |
| 2023/24 | £252 |
| 2022/23 | £252 |
| **Total (if eligible all 4 years)** | **£1,008** |

You need to have been married or in a civil partnership and eligible in each year you claim. HMRC will check.

The repayment comes as either a reduction in the receiving partner's current tax bill, or a direct payment if the credit exceeds what's owed.

<InfoCallout type="deadline" title="Don't miss the backdating window">
You can only claim back four tax years. Once a year falls outside that window, it's gone. If you think you've been eligible for a few years and haven't applied, do it now — not next tax season.
</InfoCallout>

---

## How to apply

1. Go to GOV.UK and search "apply for marriage allowance"
2. Log in with your personal tax account (or create one)
3. The lower-earning partner applies — not the higher earner
4. HMRC adjusts the receiving partner's tax code automatically
5. Backdated years are processed separately and paid as a lump sum

The whole process takes five minutes. You don't need an accountant, though if your tax affairs are complex it's worth a quick check first.

---

## When to cancel marriage allowance

Once applied, marriage allowance renews automatically every year.

You must cancel it if:
- Either of you becomes a higher-rate taxpayer (income over £50,270)
- You separate or divorce
- The lower-earning partner's income rises above £12,570

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Cancel it if your income changes">
If you forget to cancel marriage allowance when circumstances change — particularly if the receiving partner moves into higher-rate tax — you may owe money back to HMRC. Cancel through your personal tax account as soon as you know your income has changed.
</InfoCallout>

## Marriage allowance and payments on account

If you're a sole trader who makes payments on account (advance tax payments in January and July), a low-profit year has a secondary benefit: you can apply to reduce your payments on account too.

If your 2025/26 profit is much lower than 2024/25, your payments on account — which are based on last year's bill — may be too high. You can ask HMRC to reduce them.

Combine a marriage allowance claim with reduced payments on account in a low-profit year, and you can significantly improve your cash position. These are two separate actions, but they work well together.
