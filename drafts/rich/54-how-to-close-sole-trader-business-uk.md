## The short version

Closing a sole trader business isn't complicated — but it requires several separate steps, each with its own deadline. Miss one and you can end up with penalties, outstanding obligations, or tax that's harder to recover.

The steps are:

1. Notify HMRC you've stopped trading
2. File your final Self Assessment return (or MTD end of period statement)
3. Pay any outstanding tax
4. Cancel your VAT registration if you're VAT registered
5. Deregister from CIS if applicable
6. Cancel any PAYE scheme if you employed staff
7. Keep records for the required period

This guide walks through each one.

---

## Step 1: Notify HMRC you've stopped trading

Do this first. Your obligations with HMRC formally continue until you tell them you've ceased.

You can notify HMRC:
- Online via your personal tax account at GOV.UK
- By calling the Self Assessment helpline: 0300 200 3310

You'll need to confirm your final trading date. HMRC will update your record, issue any outstanding return obligations, and remove you from the self-employed system once everything is settled.

Don't assume HMRC will know automatically. They won't.

---

## Step 2: File your final return

HMRC will tell you what returns remain outstanding once you've notified them of your cessation date.

**Under standard Self Assessment:**

You'll need to file a return for the period from 6 April of the current tax year up to your last trading date. This is a normal Self Assessment return — income, expenses, profit, tax owed.

**If you're already in MTD for Income Tax:**

You must complete:
1. Your final quarterly update (covering the last period of trading)
2. An end of period statement for your final accounting period
3. A final declaration (the MTD equivalent of the full Self Assessment return)

These three steps replace the single annual tax return under MTD. Your software should guide you through them in the correct order.

---

## Step 3: Pay your final tax bill

Your final return will calculate any tax and National Insurance owed.

Things to be aware of:

**Payments on account:** If you've been making payments on account, your final tax bill needs to be reconciled against what you've already paid. You may owe a balancing payment — or you may be owed a refund if your final-year profit was lower than your payments on account assumed.

**Final Class 4 NIC:** National Insurance contributions for sole traders are calculated through Self Assessment. Your final year's profit will determine your last Class 4 NIC liability.

**Class 2 NIC:** Class 2 has been effectively abolished from April 2024 for most traders, but may still apply in some transitional situations — check your specific position.

Pay any outstanding balance by the normal Self Assessment deadline (31 January following the end of the tax year). Late payment incurs interest and penalties.

## What to cancel and when

| Registration | Who needs to act | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| Self Assessment / sole trader registration | Everyone | Notify HMRC as soon as you stop trading |
| VAT registration | If you're VAT registered | Within 30 days of the date you stopped making VAT-taxable supplies |
| CIS (Construction Industry Scheme) | If you were registered as a subcontractor | Contact HMRC to deregister |
| PAYE scheme | If you employed staff | Final RTI submission, then contact HMRC to close the scheme |
| National Insurance (Class 2) | Most traders no longer need separate action | Handled through final Self Assessment return |

---

## Step 4: Cancel your VAT registration

If you're VAT registered, you must cancel your registration within 30 days of the date you stopped making VAT-taxable supplies.

You can deregister:
- Online via your VAT account on GOV.UK
- By submitting form VAT7

After deregistering, you must file a final VAT return covering the period up to your deregistration date and pay any VAT owed. You'll also need to account for VAT on any business assets you're keeping if you claimed VAT back on them originally — HMRC calls this a "deemed supply".

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Don't cancel VAT registration late">
The 30-day deadline for VAT deregistration is firm. Miss it and HMRC can issue a penalty. If you're unsure of your exact cessation date, contact HMRC promptly and clarify — it's better to notify early than to let the deadline pass.
</InfoCallout>

---

## Step 5 and 6: CIS and PAYE

**CIS (Construction Industry Scheme):** If you were registered as a CIS subcontractor, contact HMRC to deregister. There's no automatic deregistration when you stop trading.

**PAYE:** If you employed staff at any point, you'll need to:
1. Submit your final Full Payment Submission (FPS) through HMRC's RTI system
2. Send your final Employer Payment Summary (EPS) marked as final
3. Issue P45s to any employees
4. Contact HMRC to close your PAYE scheme

If you had no employees, skip this step entirely.

---

## Step 7: Keep your records

This step is easy to forget — and it matters.

HMRC requires you to keep your business records for **five years after the 31 January filing deadline** that follows the last tax year you were trading.

**Example:** If your final trading date is 30 September 2025 (in tax year 2025/26), your 31 January deadline is 31 January 2027. You must keep records until at least **31 January 2032**.

Records to keep:
- Invoices issued and received
- Bank statements
- Expense receipts and documentation
- Mileage logs
- Any MTD digital records
- Payroll records (if applicable)
- VAT records (if applicable)

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Five years — not the end of the tax year">
The five-year rule runs from the 31 January filing deadline, not from when you stop trading. It's longer than people expect. Don't destroy records early just because you've filed your final return.
</InfoCallout>

## Terminal loss relief: if your final year is a loss

If your sole trader business makes a loss in its final accounting period, you may be able to claim **terminal loss relief**.

Terminal loss relief lets you carry a final-year loss back up to three previous tax years, offsetting it against profits from those years. This can result in a repayment of tax you paid in those years.

The loss must arise in the final 12 months of trading. It's claimed on your final Self Assessment return (box 76 on the SA103 self-employment supplementary pages).

**Example:** Your final year produces a £4,000 loss. You had profits of £5,000 in 2022/23 and £3,000 in 2023/24. You can carry back the loss and claim a tax refund for the excess tax you paid in those years.

Don't overlook this. It's a legitimate relief that's worth claiming if you're closing a business that's been struggling.

## Business assets: what to do when you close

If you're keeping any business assets for personal use when you close — a laptop, a camera, a vehicle — HMRC treats this as a disposal at market value on the date of cessation.

This means:
- If you claimed the asset's cost as an expense (through the annual investment allowance or capital allowances), HMRC will calculate a "balancing charge" — additional taxable income — if the market value exceeds the remaining tax written-down value
- If you haven't fully depreciated the asset for tax purposes, this can trigger a small extra tax liability

For most sole traders with modest equipment, the figures are small. But if you have significant assets — a commercial vehicle, specialist equipment, or property you used in the business — get advice before closing.

---

## Closing a business that was in MTD

If you were already live on MTD for Income Tax when you decided to close:

1. Continue submitting quarterly updates as normal until your cessation date
2. Submit your final quarterly update, covering the period up to your last trading date
3. Complete your end of period statement for the final period
4. Submit your final declaration

Your MTD-compatible software should handle the mechanics of marking a period as final. If you're unsure, contact your software provider — most have specific guidance for business closure scenarios.

## Quick summary

- Notify HMRC you've stopped trading — do this first
- File your final Self Assessment return (or MTD final declaration)
- Pay any outstanding tax, including payments on account reconciliation
- Cancel VAT registration within 30 days if you're VAT registered
- Deregister from CIS and close PAYE scheme if applicable
- Keep records for five years after the 31 January deadline following your last trading year
- If you made a loss in your final year, claim terminal loss relief — it could mean a tax refund
- If you're keeping business assets, check whether a disposal charge applies
