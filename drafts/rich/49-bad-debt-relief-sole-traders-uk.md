## When a client doesn't pay

A client goes quiet. An invoice ages past 60, then 90 days. You exhaust your options and accept the debt is unrecoverable.

The financial loss is bad enough. But there is also a tax angle most sole traders miss: if you already recorded that invoice as income and paid (or owe) tax on it, you may be able to get that tax back.

This is bad debt relief — and whether it applies to you depends entirely on which accounting method you use.

---

## The key question: accruals or cash basis?

Your accounting method determines everything here.

| Accounting method | When income is recorded | Bad debt relief needed? |
|------------------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Cash basis | When payment is received | No — unrecorded income creates no tax liability |
| Accruals | When invoice is issued | Yes — tax may already be owed on unpaid invoices |

Most sole traders in the UK use **cash basis** — HMRC's default for self-employed people with turnover under £150,000. Under cash basis, you only record income when money hits your account.

If a client never pays, that invoice never appears in your accounts. No income recorded. No tax to recover. Bad debt is still financially painful, but it creates no tax problem.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Most sole traders using cash basis are not affected by bad debt tax issues">
If you use cash basis accounting — which is the default for most UK sole traders — unpaid invoices never created a tax liability in the first place. You have a cash flow problem, not a tax problem. Bad debt relief as a tax concept is primarily relevant to those on accruals accounting.
</InfoCallout>

---

## Accruals accounting and bad debt relief

Under **accruals accounting**, income is recorded when the invoice is raised — not when it is paid. This means if you issued an invoice in March, that income appears in your accounts for that tax year, even if the client has not paid.

If that client then fails to pay, you have:
- Declared the income
- Potentially paid (or owe) tax on it
- Received nothing

Bad debt relief allows you to reverse that by writing off the debt as an allowable expense in the year you determine it is unrecoverable.

---

## HMRC requirements for bad debt relief

HMRC does not let you claim relief simply because an invoice is overdue. You must be able to demonstrate:

1. **The debt is genuinely bad** — not just slow to pay, but actually unrecoverable. Administration of the client company, confirmed non-payment after legal proceedings, or similar.
2. **The debt has been formally written off** in your accounts — you must have taken the deliberate accounting step of writing it off, with a date.
3. **You made reasonable attempts to recover the debt** — reminder letters, formal demands, and (for significant amounts) legal steps.

HMRC will not accept a bad debt claim where you simply decided not to chase. The write-off must be genuine and documented.

---

## How bad debt relief works in practice

### Step 1: Exhaust reasonable recovery options

Send a final demand letter. For significant amounts, consider a letter before action or formal legal proceedings. Document every step in writing.

### Step 2: Formally write off the debt

Record the write-off in your accounts. Note the date. Reduce your debtors balance and create a bad debt expense entry.

### Step 3: Include on your Self Assessment return

On your Self Assessment return, include the written-off amount in your allowable business expenses. This reduces your taxable profit and recovers the tax you would otherwise pay on income you never received.

---

## Cash basis and bad debts: what you still need to know

Even if cash basis protects you from the main bad debt tax problem, there are two scenarios where you need to pay attention:

### Recovering a previously written-off debt

If you previously wrote off a debt (perhaps you recorded it differently, or you are transitioning between methods), and the client later pays — you must declare that payment as income in the year you receive it.

### Switching from accruals to cash basis

If you previously used accruals accounting and switch to cash basis, any outstanding debtors need to be handled carefully. Outstanding invoices that you already recorded as income under accruals should not be double-counted under cash basis. Get advice if you are making this switch.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="VAT bad debt relief is a completely separate process">
If you are VAT registered and have paid output VAT to HMRC on an invoice that remains unpaid for more than 6 months, you can reclaim that VAT through HMRC's VAT bad debt relief scheme. This is separate from income tax bad debt relief and requires its own claim process. Speak to your accountant if this applies to you.
</InfoCallout>

## Cash basis vs accruals: bad debt comparison table

| Scenario | Cash basis | Accruals |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| Client doesn't pay | Income never recorded — no tax issue | Income recorded — tax potentially owed |
| Can claim bad debt relief? | No (not needed) | Yes — write off as allowable expense |
| Client later pays a written-off debt | Declare as income when received | Declare as income when received |
| VAT bad debt relief | Separate — applies if VAT registered | Separate — applies if VAT registered |
| Record-keeping required | Basic payment records | Detailed debtor records, write-off documentation |

## Worked example: consultant on accruals accounting

James is a management consultant using accruals accounting. He invoices a client £5,000 in October 2025. The client goes into administration in February 2026 without paying.

Under accruals, James recorded the £5,000 as income in 2025/26. He now faces a tax bill on income he never received.

James:
1. Issues a formal debt recovery letter (documents it)
2. Receives confirmation that the client is in administration with no prospect of recovery
3. Formally writes off the £5,000 debt in his accounts in March 2026
4. Includes £5,000 as a bad debt expense on his 2025/26 Self Assessment return

**Result:** His taxable profit reduces by £5,000. At the 20% basic rate tax band, he saves **£1,000 in tax**.

If James had been using cash basis, none of this would be necessary — the £5,000 would simply never have appeared as income.

---

## What this means for choosing your accounting method

For most sole traders, cash basis is simpler and removes the bad debt tax problem entirely. But some businesses are required to use accruals (those with turnover over £150,000, or those choosing it for specific reasons such as working with an accountant or needing more detailed financial reporting).

If you are on accruals, bad debt relief is a legitimate tool. Use it properly — document the write-off, evidence your attempts to collect, and include it on your return.
