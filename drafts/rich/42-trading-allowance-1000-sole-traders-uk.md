## The short version

HMRC gives every UK individual a £1,000 trading allowance each tax year. If your total self-employment or casual trading income is £1,000 or less, you pay no tax on it and don't need to register for Self Assessment.

If your income exceeds £1,000, you need to register and report it — but you can still use the allowance to reduce what you're taxed on, provided you don't also claim expenses.

That last point is where people get tripped up. Trading allowance and expenses are mutually exclusive. You pick one. You cannot have both.

---

## Who the trading allowance is for

The trading allowance is designed for people who earn casual or occasional income outside of a formal salaried job. Think:

- Selling secondhand items on eBay or Vinted
- Making and selling crafts on Etsy
- Dog walking, babysitting, or pet sitting
- Occasional tutoring or music lessons
- Renting out a parking space or driveway
- Earning from odd jobs or handyman work
- Generating small amounts from a blog, YouTube, or social media

If any of these describes you, the trading allowance may mean you have no tax liability at all — and potentially no need to even register with HMRC.

---

## The critical rule: allowance OR expenses, not both

This is the most important thing to understand about the trading allowance.

When you earn more than £1,000 in trading income, you have a choice about how you reduce your taxable profit:

**Option A — Claim the trading allowance:** Deduct a flat £1,000 from your gross income. No receipts required. Simple.

**Option B — Claim actual expenses:** Deduct your real, documented business costs from your gross income.

You cannot combine them. Once you choose, that's your method for that tax year for that income source.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="You cannot claim both">
The trading allowance and business expense claims are mutually exclusive. If you elect to use the trading allowance, you give up your right to deduct any actual expenses against that income — and vice versa. Always calculate which gives you the bigger deduction before deciding.
</InfoCallout>

## Decision table: trading allowance vs expenses

| Your situation | Use trading allowance | Claim expenses |
|---|---|---|
| Total trading income under £1,000 | Yes — no tax due | Not needed |
| Expenses are less than £1,000 | Yes — allowance gives more relief | No |
| Expenses equal exactly £1,000 | Either — same result | Either |
| Expenses are more than £1,000 | No | Yes — claim expenses |
| You want simplicity, fewer records | Yes | No |
| You have detailed records already | Either | Yes, if expenses > £1,000 |
| Income is high, expenses are high | No | Yes |

The rule of thumb: if your expenses exceed £1,000, claim expenses. If they don't, the allowance wins.

## Worked example 1: Etsy seller, £800 income

Sarah sells handmade candles on Etsy. In 2025/26, she earns £800 in total.

Her trading income is under £1,000, so:
- The trading allowance covers her entirely
- She pays no tax on this income
- She does not need to register for Self Assessment for this income source

Simple. Done.

---

## Worked example 2: eBay seller, £3,000 income, £600 expenses

Marcus sells vintage clothing on eBay. In 2025/26 he earns £3,000. His actual business expenses — postage, packaging, eBay fees — come to £600.

He now has two options:

**Option A — Trading allowance:**
- £3,000 income minus £1,000 allowance = £2,000 taxable profit

**Option B — Claim expenses:**
- £3,000 income minus £600 expenses = £2,400 taxable profit

The trading allowance gives him a bigger deduction. He should use the allowance.

---

## Worked example 3: Freelancer, £3,000 income, £1,500 expenses

Priya does occasional copywriting alongside her main job. She earns £3,000 from it in 2025/26. Her business costs — software subscriptions, a proportion of her phone bill, professional development — total £1,500.

**Option A — Trading allowance:**
- £3,000 minus £1,000 = £2,000 taxable profit

**Option B — Claim expenses:**
- £3,000 minus £1,500 = £1,500 taxable profit

Expenses win here. Priya should claim her actual costs. She'll pay tax on £500 less income.

## What happens when your income goes over £1,000

The threshold is your trigger point for Self Assessment registration — not for how much tax you pay.

If your trading income in a tax year exceeds £1,000:

1. You must register for Self Assessment with HMRC
2. You must file a tax return for that year
3. You can still use the trading allowance to reduce your taxable profit (as long as you don't also claim expenses)
4. You will only pay tax on income above the allowance (and above your Personal Allowance)

So it's possible to earn £1,500 in trading income, use the £1,000 allowance, and owe little or no income tax — but you still need to be registered and file a return.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Property income has a separate allowance">
If you earn both trading income and rental income, there are two separate £1,000 allowances — one for each. The property income allowance works the same way: tax-free up to £1,000, and you must choose between the allowance or deducting actual property expenses. They do not interfere with each other.
</InfoCallout>

## Common mistakes to avoid

**1. Thinking you're safe from registration because your tax bill is zero**

Your total gross trading income determines whether you need to register — not your tax liability after allowances. If you earned £1,200, you register. Full stop.

**2. Trying to claim both the allowance and expenses**

HMRC will not allow this. If you file a return claiming both, it will be rejected or trigger a compliance check.

**3. Confusing the trading allowance with the personal allowance**

These are completely different. The personal allowance (£12,570 in 2025/26) is the amount of total income before you pay income tax. The trading allowance is specific to trading/self-employment income and sits on top of the normal tax rules.

**4. Forgetting the allowance applies to gross income, not profit**

You apply the trading allowance to your gross trading income before calculating any deductions. If you have £900 income and £200 in business costs, you don't need to worry — the allowance covers the gross income entirely.
