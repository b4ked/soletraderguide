This guide is written specifically for:

You have a particular MTD profile: income is often concentrated (few clients, high-value invoices), expenses include a mix of home office, equipment, subscriptions, and professional development, and IR35 may be relevant to some contracts.

This guide covers what MTD means for you, what you need to do, and which software actually fits how consultants work.

## What MTD for Income Tax actually means

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment (MTD ITSA) replaces the annual Self Assessment tax return with a quarterly digital reporting process.

Under MTD, you will:

1. **Keep digital records** of all income and expenses throughout the year
2. **Submit quarterly updates** to HMRC — a summary of income and expenses for each quarter
3. **Submit an End of Period Statement (EOPS)** at the end of the tax year — confirming your full year figures
4. **Submit a Final Declaration** (replacing the Self Assessment return) to calculate your total tax liability

The quarterly updates are not detailed accounts — they are summaries. Your accounting software generates and submits them. The process is more administrative than the current annual return, but software handles most of it.

<InfoCallout type="deadline" title="Sign up before your mandatory start date — do not wait">
HMRC requires eligible taxpayers to sign up for MTD ITSA before their start date. If your qualifying income exceeds £50,000, your start date is April 2026. If between £30,000 and £50,000, it is April 2027. Sign up early — your software provider will guide you through the process, but leave enough time before your first quarterly deadline.
</InfoCallout>

## Does MTD apply to you?

MTD ITSA applies if your **qualifying income** exceeds the relevant threshold.

| Qualifying income | MTD start date |
|------------------|---------------|
| Over £50,000 | April 2026 |
| £30,000 – £50,000 | April 2027 |
| £20,000 – £30,000 | To be confirmed (likely 2028) |
| Under £20,000 | No current obligation |

**Qualifying income** is your **gross turnover** from self-employment and/or UK property — not your profit after expenses.

If you invoiced £55,000 last year and had £18,000 of expenses, your qualifying income is £55,000. You are in scope.

## IR35 and MTD: an important interaction

IR35 affects whether your income counts as self-employment income or employment income.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="IR35 changes what counts as qualifying income for MTD">
Income treated as employment income under IR35 is NOT self-employment income for MTD purposes. If all your work is inside IR35, that income may not count as qualifying income for MTD ITSA. However, if you have a mix of inside and outside IR35 contracts, only your genuinely self-employed income counts toward the threshold. This can be complex — speak to an accountant if you have mixed IR35 status.
</InfoCallout>

In practice:

- **Fully outside IR35** (true sole trader): All your income is qualifying income for MTD
- **Fully inside IR35**: Your income is treated as employment — likely falls outside MTD ITSA scope (check with an accountant)
- **Mixed status**: Only the outside-IR35 self-employment income counts toward the qualifying income threshold

## Key expenses for consultants — what to record

Consultants typically have simple income (a handful of clients, invoices for services) but more varied expense claims. Here is what you should be recording:

### Home office

You can claim a proportion of home costs for the space you use for work:
- **Flat rate method**: HMRC's simplified expenses — £10, £18, or £26/month depending on how many hours you work from home
- **Actual costs method**: Apportion mortgage interest/rent, utilities, council tax, insurance by the proportion of your home used and the proportion of time used for business

Most sole traders who work mostly from home find the actual costs method yields a larger deduction, but it requires more record-keeping.

### Equipment and technology

- Laptop and desktop computers
- Monitors, keyboards, peripherals
- Specialist software licences
- Mobile phone (business proportion)

### Professional subscriptions and memberships

- Industry body memberships
- Professional journal subscriptions
- LinkedIn Premium if used for client development

### Training and professional development

Training that maintains or updates your existing professional skills (not acquiring new qualifications) is generally allowable.

### Travel

Travel to client sites, including:
- Mileage at HMRC's approved rate (45p/mile for first 10,000 miles, 25p/mile thereafter)
- Train and taxi costs
- Hotel and subsistence for overnight trips

**Not allowable:** commuting to a regular place of work.

### Broadband and phone

Business proportion of your broadband and phone costs. If you have a dedicated business line, 100% is claimable. If shared with personal use, apportion.

## Quarterly update deadlines

Under MTD, your four quarterly update deadlines are:

| Quarter | Period covers | Deadline |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Q1 | 6 April – 5 July | 5 August |
| Q2 | 6 July – 5 October | 5 November |
| Q3 | 6 October – 5 January | 5 February |
| Q4 | 6 January – 5 April | 5 May |

Your software should handle submission automatically. But you need to have kept clean records throughout the quarter so the numbers are accurate.

## What to do now if MTD applies to you

1. **Confirm your qualifying income** — check last year's gross turnover (not profit) against the thresholds
2. **Choose your software** — start using it now, before your MTD start date
3. **Connect your bank feed** — this saves significant time in categorising transactions
4. **Set up your expense categories** — match them to HMRC's categories now
5. **Sign up for MTD** — through your software provider before your start date
6. **Set quarterly calendar reminders** — for each update deadline

The transition is not technically complex. The main risk is leaving it too late and scrambling in the weeks before April.
