## The short version

Bookkeeping is recording what comes in and what goes out. For a sole trader, that means every payment you receive for work, and every cost you incur running the business.

You need to do this because:
1. HMRC requires it — you need accurate records for Self Assessment
2. MTD for Income Tax will require digital records and quarterly submissions from April 2026 onwards
3. You can't claim expenses you haven't recorded

The good news: basic bookkeeping is not hard. The common failure isn't confusion about accounting principles — it's leaving it too long and creating a mountain to climb at year-end.

## The core concepts

Before the practical steps, here are the terms you'll encounter — explained simply.

| Term | What it means |
|---|---|
| Income | Money received for your work or services |
| Expense | A cost incurred running your business |
| Profit | Income minus allowable expenses |
| Reconcile | Matching your records to your bank statement to make sure they agree |
| Bank feed | A live connection between your bank and your software — transactions appear automatically |
| Categorise | Assigning each transaction to a type (e.g. "professional fees", "office costs", "travel") |
| Cash basis | Recording income when received and expenses when paid |
| Accruals | Recording income when invoiced and expenses when incurred |

Most sole traders never need to go beyond understanding these seven terms. Everything else is detail.

## Recording income: what counts and when to record it

**What counts as business income:**
- Payments received for work or services
- Money from product sales
- Grants received for the business
- Any other money earned through self-employment

**What does not count:**
- Personal money you put into the business (transfers from your personal account)
- Loan proceeds
- VAT you collect (if VAT registered — this belongs to HMRC, not you)

**When to record it:**

Under **cash basis** (the default for most sole traders): record income when you receive the money. A client pays you on 15 March — you record it on 15 March.

Under **accruals** (less common, used by some): record income when you raise the invoice, even if unpaid. An invoice dated 15 March gets recorded on 15 March whether or not the client has paid yet.

Most sole traders on cash basis. It's simpler, and HMRC allows it if your turnover is below £150,000.

## Recording expenses: categories and common mistakes

HMRC allows you to deduct "wholly and exclusively" business costs from your income before calculating your taxable profit. That means the cost must have been incurred entirely for business purposes.

**Common allowable expense categories:**

- Office costs (stationery, postage, printing)
- Travel (mileage, public transport, parking — business journeys only)
- Stock and materials (goods you buy to resell or use in your work)
- Marketing and advertising
- Professional fees (accountant, solicitor)
- Software subscriptions used for work
- Phone and internet (business proportion only if also used personally)
- Training and professional development directly related to your current trade
- Bank charges on your business account

**What you cannot claim:**
- Personal living costs
- Business clothing that could also be worn casually
- Client entertaining (not allowable under HMRC rules)
- Fines and penalties

The key discipline: categorise every expense at the point of recording it. Don't leave a pile of "miscellaneous" items to sort later. If you're unsure whether something is allowable, note it separately and check.

## Bank reconciliation: what it is and why it matters

Reconciliation means comparing your bookkeeping records to your bank statement and confirming they match.

If your records show £4,200 in income for October, and your bank statement shows £4,200 in business receipts for October — you're reconciled.

If they don't match, something has been missed, duplicated, or miscategorised. Catching this monthly means a 15-minute fix. Catching it at year-end means hours of archaeology.

**How to reconcile:**
1. Download or open your bank statement for the month
2. Go through it line by line against your records
3. Tick off each transaction as you match it
4. Anything unmatched in your bank statement needs to be added to your records
5. Anything in your records not on the statement needs to be investigated

If you use a bank feed in your accounting software, reconciliation is largely automated — the software matches transactions to your records as they come in, and you just confirm or categorise the ones it flags.

## How often should you do it?

| Frequency | Time required | Who it suits |
|---|---|---|
| Daily | 5–10 minutes | High-volume traders, those with many transactions |
| Weekly | 10–20 minutes | Most sole traders — best practice |
| Monthly | 30–60 minutes | Minimum recommended frequency |
| Quarterly | 2–4 hours | Only workable with very few transactions |
| Annually | A very bad day | Not recommended — errors compound |

Under MTD for Income Tax, you'll need to submit quarterly updates — which means your records must be reasonably current every three months at minimum. Weekly bookkeeping makes quarterly submissions a five-minute job. Annual bookkeeping makes them a crisis.

<InfoCallout type="tip" title="MTD is actually forcing a good habit">
Quarterly submissions under MTD might feel like an imposition. But sole traders who adopt regular bookkeeping — weekly or monthly — consistently report less year-end stress, faster decision-making, and better awareness of their tax liability throughout the year. The discipline MTD requires is one that benefits you anyway.
</InfoCallout>

## The three most common bookkeeping mistakes

**1. Mixing personal and business finances**

This is the number one error. If personal purchases appear in your business account — or business costs get paid from your personal account — your records become unreliable and your tax return becomes an exercise in forensic reconstruction.

Open a separate business bank account. It doesn't need to be an expensive "business" account — a separate personal account used solely for business is enough. Keep them apart from day one.

**2. Not keeping receipts**

You cannot claim an expense without evidence that you incurred it. HMRC can ask you to substantiate any deduction. If you can't produce a receipt or invoice for a cost, you may lose the deduction in an enquiry.

Under MTD, the transaction must be in your digital records. The receipt is your supporting evidence. Both matter.

**3. Leaving it until year-end**

A full year of unrecorded transactions is genuinely hard to reconstruct. Bank statements tell you amounts, not what things were for. Receipts get lost. You forget which expenses were business and which were personal. You miss legitimate deductions because you can't find the evidence.

Monthly bookkeeping prevents all of this. It's one of the highest-leverage habits a sole trader can build.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Keep personal and business finances completely separate">
Open a separate account for your business — even if it's just a separate personal account. It takes 20 minutes to set up and saves hours every year. Mixing personal and business transactions is the single biggest source of bookkeeping errors and missed expense claims.
</InfoCallout>

## A simple monthly bookkeeping routine

This four-step routine takes 30 to 60 minutes per month for most sole traders:

**Step 1 — Log income** (10–20 minutes)
Go through your bank statement or invoice system and record every payment received during the month. Date, amount, and what it was for.

**Step 2 — Log expenses** (10–20 minutes)
Go through your bank statements and any paper receipts. Record every business cost. Categorise each one. Check nothing business-related was paid from your personal account.

**Step 3 — Reconcile** (5–10 minutes)
Compare your records to your bank statement. Confirm everything matches. Investigate anything that doesn't.

**Step 4 — File your receipts** (5 minutes)
Whether digitally or in a folder, make sure the supporting evidence for every transaction is stored somewhere you can find it.

That's it. Once a month. Consistent.

## Bookkeeping under MTD: what changes

MTD for Income Tax requires digital records and quarterly submissions. In practice this means:

- Your bookkeeping must be in digital form (software or connected spreadsheet)
- Each transaction must be recorded individually — no monthly totals
- Every three months, you submit a summary of your income and expenses to HMRC directly from your software
- At year-end, you submit a final declaration (replacing the traditional Self Assessment return)

Good bookkeeping habits — weekly recording, monthly reconciliation — make quarterly MTD submissions straightforward. Poor habits — batch entry, missing transactions — make them painful and create compliance risk.

The traders who find MTD easiest are those who were already doing regular bookkeeping. If that's not you yet, the best time to start is now — before MTD forces the issue.
