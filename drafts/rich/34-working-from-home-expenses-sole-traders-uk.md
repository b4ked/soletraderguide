# Working From Home Expenses for UK Sole Traders: Flat Rate vs Actual Costs Explained

If you run your business from home — even part of the time — HMRC allows you to claim a proportion of your household running costs as a business expense. That reduces your taxable profit, which reduces your tax bill.

But there are two different ways to calculate what you can claim, and choosing the right method can make a meaningful difference to how much you save.

This guide explains both methods clearly, gives you a real worked example comparing them, and helps you decide which is right for your situation.

---

## The two HMRC methods

HMRC gives sole traders two options for claiming home working expenses:

1. **Flat rate (simplified expenses)** — a set monthly amount based on how many hours you work from home, no receipts required
2. **Actual costs method** — a calculated proportion of your real household bills, based on the part of the home you use and the hours you work there

Both are legitimate. Neither is automatically better. It depends on your household costs, how much of your home you use for work, and how much time you spend working there.

---

## Method 1: Flat rate (simplified expenses)

HMRC's simplified expenses scheme lets you claim a fixed monthly amount without needing to track or apportion individual bills.

### HMRC flat rates for working from home (2025/26)

| Hours worked from home per month | Monthly flat rate |
|---|---|
| 25 to 50 hours | £10/month |
| 51 to 100 hours | £18/month |
| 101 hours or more | £26/month |

These rates are per month, not per year. If you work 60 hours from home in January but only 20 in February, you claim £18 for January and nothing for February (you must exceed 25 hours to claim anything in a given month).

### Who the flat rate suits

- Sole traders with modest household bills
- People who work from home part-time or irregularly
- Anyone who wants a simple, low-admin approach
- Those who prefer not to keep detailed records of household costs

### What you cannot claim alongside the flat rate

If you use simplified expenses for home working, you cannot also claim the actual costs of household bills for the same property. It is one method or the other.

You can still claim other business expenses separately — for example, a dedicated business phone line, or office equipment you bought solely for work.

---

## Method 2: Actual costs

The actual costs method allows you to claim a calculated proportion of your real household running costs. It takes more work, but often produces a higher deduction — particularly if you have high bills or use a significant portion of your home for work.

### What you can include

- Gas and electricity (heating and lighting)
- Water rates (if applicable to your work use)
- Broadband (work-use proportion only)
- Rent (work-use proportion)
- Mortgage interest — not capital repayments (work-use proportion)
- Council tax (work-use proportion)
- Home insurance (work-use proportion)
- General repairs and maintenance (work-use proportion)

### What you cannot include

- The full cost of any bill — you must always apportion
- Capital repayments on a mortgage (only interest is allowable)
- Personal share of any cost
- Improvements to the property (these may create a capital gains tax issue on sale — seek advice)

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Council tax and renting">
If you rent your home and claim a proportion of council tax as a business expense, HMRC accepts this as legitimate under the actual costs method. However, if you own your home, claiming council tax can complicate your Capital Gains Tax position when you sell — a portion of your home may no longer qualify for Private Residence Relief. Take advice before including council tax in a large home working claim.
</InfoCallout>

### How to calculate your work-use proportion

The standard approach is to divide by:

1. **Rooms**: count the number of rooms in your home (excluding bathrooms and halls). Then identify how many are used for work. If you use one room exclusively for work in a four-room home, the room proportion is 25%.

2. **Hours**: if the room is not used exclusively for work (for example, your dining table doubles as a desk), also factor in the proportion of time it is used for work. If the room is used for work 40 hours a week out of 168 hours total, that is roughly 24%.

3. **Combined**: multiply the two proportions together. In the above example: 25% × 24% = 6% of total household bills.

This can feel complicated, but the key principle is that the claim must be reasonable and defensible. HMRC does not specify a single formula — it wants you to use a fair and consistent method.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="HMRC audit risk for large home claims">
Claims that represent a high proportion of household bills attract scrutiny. If you are claiming 50% or more of your home costs as business use, make sure you have clear records that justify it — room count, hours worked, nature of the work. An accountant's input is worthwhile for large claims.
</InfoCallout>

## Phone and broadband

Phone and broadband are worth addressing separately because they come up so often.

- If you have a **dedicated business phone line or mobile**, you can claim 100% of that cost
- If you use a **personal phone or broadband package** for both work and personal use, you can only claim the **business-use proportion**
- There is no set formula — HMRC wants a reasonable estimate. Many sole traders claim 50–80% of their broadband bill if they work from home full-time

Keep a record of how you arrived at your proportion in case HMRC asks.

## Worked example: 60 hours per month from home

Let us take a real scenario and run the numbers under both methods.

**Sarah is a freelance graphic designer.** She works 60 hours per month from home in a spare bedroom she uses exclusively as a studio. The rest of the house has three other rooms.

**Annual household costs:**
- Gas and electricity: £1,800/year
- Broadband: £360/year
- Council tax: £1,500/year
- Rent: £12,000/year

**Total: £15,660/year**

### Method 1: Flat rate

60 hours per month puts Sarah in the £18/month bracket.

**Annual claim: £18 × 12 = £216**

### Method 2: Actual costs

Sarah uses 1 room out of 4 exclusively for work (25% room proportion). The room is used for work only, so she does not need to apply a further hours adjustment.

She claims 25% of all eligible costs.

| Cost | Annual | 25% claim |
|---|---|---|
| Gas and electricity | £1,800 | £450 |
| Broadband (80% work use) | £360 | £288 |
| Council tax | £1,500 | £375 |
| Rent | £12,000 | £3,000 |
| **Total** | **£15,660** | **£4,113** |

**Annual claim (actual costs): £4,113**

### Comparison

| Method | Annual claim | Tax saved (basic rate, 20%) | Tax saved (higher rate, 40%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Flat rate | £216 | £43 | £86 |
| Actual costs | £4,113 | £823 | £1,645 |

In Sarah's case, the actual costs method delivers dramatically more tax relief. But she has a dedicated room and significant rent — those factors make her situation ideal for the actual costs method.

## Which method should you use?

| Situation | Better method |
|---|---|
| You work from home occasionally or part-time | Flat rate |
| Your household bills are low | Flat rate |
| You share your working space with personal use | Flat rate (simpler to defend) |
| You have a dedicated room used mainly for work | Actual costs |
| You have high rent, mortgage interest, or energy bills | Actual costs |
| You work from home full-time | Actual costs (usually) |
| You want minimal admin | Flat rate |
| You want to maximise your deductions | Actual costs |

## Key rules to remember

- You must be genuinely working from home — occasional home working counts, but you cannot claim if you only have a home address and actually work elsewhere
- You can switch between the flat rate and actual costs method from year to year, but you cannot use both in the same tax year
- Keep records for at least five years after the relevant tax return filing deadline
- If you use your home as your main place of business AND as personal accommodation, you need to apply a reasonable apportionment — claiming 100% of any bill is almost never justifiable
