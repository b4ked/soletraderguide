## Before you start

Self Assessment is not complicated if you're organised. The stress usually comes from leaving it to January or not knowing what to expect.

This guide walks you through the process from start to finish, explains the forms involved, flags the common mistakes, and tells you how MTD changes things from April 2026 onward.

---

## What you need to gather first

Before you log in to Government Gateway, collect:

| What | Where to find it |
|------|-----------------|
| UTR (Unique Taxpayer Reference) | Original HMRC registration letter, or Government Gateway account |
| National Insurance number | Payslip, P60, NI card, or any HMRC letter |
| Income records | Sales invoices, bank statements, PayPal/Stripe reports |
| Expense records | Receipts, supplier invoices, mileage log |
| Bank statements | Useful for cross-checking income and expenses |
| P60 (if employed too) | From your employer, covers April–April |
| Previous year's tax return | Useful for reference, especially payments on account |
| Student loan details | If you're still repaying — the return will ask |

The more organised your records are before you start, the faster the return takes. Good accounting software (or even a well-maintained spreadsheet) means you can pull these figures in minutes.

---

## Step 1: Register for Self Assessment (if you haven't already)

If this is your first return, you need to register before you can file.

**Deadline:** Register by 5 October in the second tax year of self-employment. So if you started trading in 2024/25, register by 5 October 2025.

You register through [HMRC's Government Gateway](https://www.gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment). Once registered, HMRC will send your UTR in the post — this can take up to 10 working days, so don't leave it late.

<InfoCallout type="deadline" title="Register by 5 October">
If you started self-employment in the 2024/25 tax year, your deadline to register for Self Assessment is 5 October 2025. Missing this can result in a penalty — register as soon as you know you need to.
</InfoCallout>

---

## Step 2: Log in to Government Gateway

Go to [gov.uk/log-in-file-self-assessment-tax-return](https://www.gov.uk/log-in-file-self-assessment-tax-return) and sign in with your Government Gateway ID and password.

If you've lost your login details, HMRC has a recovery process — but it can take several days, so don't start this in late January.

---

## Step 3: Complete the SA100 (main tax return)

The SA100 is the core self assessment form. It covers:

- your personal details and circumstances
- whether you had UK income (employment, pension, savings, property)
- whether you need to complete any supplementary pages
- your personal allowance and tax calculation

For most sole traders, the SA100 itself is brief. The detail lives in the supplementary pages.

---

## Step 4: Complete the SA103 (self-employment income)

The SA103 is the supplementary page for self-employment income. This is where the substance of your return lives.

**Key boxes explained:**

| Box | What it covers |
|-----|---------------|
| Turnover | Total business income before expenses — what you invoiced and received |
| Allowable expenses | What you can deduct — costs wholly and exclusively for business |
| Net profit | Turnover minus allowable expenses — this is what gets taxed |
| Capital allowances | For equipment and assets you've bought |
| Goods/services for personal use | If you've taken anything from the business for personal use |

**Common allowable expenses for sole traders:**
- office costs (stationery, printer ink, phone used for work)
- travel costs (fuel, train fares, mileage at HMRC's approved rate)
- marketing (website, ads)
- software subscriptions
- professional indemnity insurance
- accountant fees
- training directly relevant to your trade

**Not allowable:**
- client entertainment
- clothing (unless a protective uniform)
- personal phone costs (only the business proportion)
- home broadband if you also use it personally (only the business proportion)

---

## Step 5: Check your tax calculation

Once you've entered your figures, the HMRC system calculates your tax bill automatically. Review it before submitting.

For 2025/26:
- Personal allowance: £12,570 (no tax below this)
- Basic rate: 20% on profit between £12,570 and £50,270
- Higher rate: 40% on profit between £50,270 and £125,140
- Class 4 National Insurance: 6% on profits between £12,570 and £50,270, 2% above

The calculation also shows:
- any tax already paid (e.g., through PAYE if you're employed)
- payments on account you've already made
- what you owe (or are owed back)

---

## Step 6: Understand payments on account

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="The payment on account trap">
First-time Self Assessment filers are frequently blindsided by this. In January, you don't just pay the tax you owe for the year just ended. HMRC also collects the first payment on account for the coming year — calculated as 50% of your previous year's bill. So if you owe £2,000 for 2024/25, you pay £2,000 + £1,000 in January = £3,000. The second payment on account (another £1,000) is due 31 July.
</InfoCallout>

Payments on account apply if your previous year's tax bill (excluding Class 2 NI) was over £1,000 and you paid less than 80% at source (e.g., through PAYE).

If your income has dropped significantly, you can apply to reduce your payments on account — but do this before the deadline, not after.

---

## Step 7: Submit and pay

Once you're satisfied with the return, submit it through Government Gateway.

**Online filing deadline:** 31 January (for the previous tax year ending 5 April)
**Payment deadline:** Also 31 January

You can pay by:
- online banking (bank transfer to HMRC's account)
- debit card through Government Gateway
- direct debit (if set up in advance)

Keep your submission reference. HMRC will send a confirmation email.

## Common mistakes to avoid

| Mistake | What to do instead |
|---------|-------------------|
| Forgetting payments on account | Budget for 150% of your estimated annual bill in January |
| Claiming non-allowable expenses | When in doubt, check HMRC's list or ask an accountant |
| Wrong tax year dates (it's 6 April to 5 April, not calendar year) | Check your dates carefully |
| Late registration (missing 5 October) | Register as soon as you start trading |
| Not claiming all allowable expenses | Keep receipts and use accounting software |
| Missing the filing deadline entirely | File even if you can't pay — the penalty for late filing is separate from late payment |

---

## What MTD changes from April 2026

<InfoCallout type="info" title="MTD replaces Self Assessment for self-employment income">
From April 2026, if your qualifying income exceeds £50,000, you'll need to submit quarterly updates to HMRC instead of a single annual return for your self-employment income. A final declaration replaces the SA return for that income stream. However, Self Assessment may still apply for other income types. The transition is being phased in — check your eligibility.
</InfoCallout>

Under MTD for Income Tax Self Assessment:
- quarterly digital records submitted to HMRC (April, July, October, January)
- a final declaration at year end (replacing the SA103 for self-employment)
- requires HMRC-recognised software — spreadsheets alone won't be enough

The 2025/26 tax year is likely to be one of the last where most self-employed people file a standard SA return for their business income. Start getting your records in order now.
