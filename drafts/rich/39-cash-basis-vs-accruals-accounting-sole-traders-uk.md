## The short answer

Use **cash basis**. It's simpler, it's the default under MTD for Income Tax, and for the vast majority of UK sole traders under £300,000 turnover, it's the right choice.

Accruals accounting exists for situations cash basis can't handle cleanly — complex stock, work-in-progress, property income above certain thresholds. If none of that applies to you, you don't need to think about it much further.

But understanding the difference is worth five minutes of your time.

---

## What cash basis accounting means

Under **cash basis**, you record:
- income when the money lands in your bank account
- expenses when you actually pay them

That's it. You track real money flows. An invoice you sent in December but haven't been paid for yet doesn't count as income until January when it's paid.

This is how most people naturally think about their finances, which is why it's the default.

---

## What accruals (traditional) accounting means

Under **accruals accounting**, you record:
- income when it's earned (i.e., when you invoice), regardless of when you're paid
- expenses when they're incurred, regardless of when you pay them

An invoice sent on 30 December goes into your December accounts — even if the client pays in February. A supplier bill received in March is a March expense — even if you pay it in April.

This gives a more accurate picture of business performance in any given period. It matches revenue to the costs that generated it. But it's more complex to track and reconcile.

---

## Worked example: the same transaction, two methods

**Scenario:** You complete a project for a client in December 2025. You invoice £2,000 on 20 December. The client pays on 15 January 2026.

| | Cash Basis | Accruals |
|--|-----------|---------|
| When is £2,000 recorded as income? | January 2026 (when paid) | December 2025 (when invoiced) |
| Which tax year does it fall in? | 2025/26 | 2025/26 (same in this case) |
| What if payment slipped to April 6? | 2026/27 tax year | Still 2025/26 |

The tax year boundary is where this gets important. Under cash basis, a late-paying client can shift income into a different tax year — which may reduce your tax bill, or complicate it, depending on your circumstances. Under accruals, the income is locked to when you earned it.

## When accruals makes sense for sole traders

Most sole traders won't need accruals. But there are situations where it either makes sense or is required:

**Turnover over £300,000.** Cash basis has an upper limit. Once you exceed £300,000 in annual turnover, you must use accruals.

**Significant stock or work-in-progress.** If you hold stock or have long-running projects with costs that precede income, accruals gives you a more accurate view of profitability.

**Property income above certain thresholds.** Landlords with property income may be required to use accruals depending on the nature and scale of their property business.

**Your accountant recommends it.** Some accountants prefer accruals even for smaller sole traders, particularly if you have a mix of income types or complex expense patterns. If your accountant has a specific reason, take their advice.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Cash basis is the default under MTD">
When Making Tax Digital for Income Tax comes into force, cash basis will be the default accounting method for sole traders. You can opt into accruals, but you have to actively choose it. Most people won't need to.
</InfoCallout>

## How this affects MTD quarterly reporting

Under MTD for Income Tax Self Assessment, you'll submit quarterly updates to HMRC covering your income and expenses for each quarter.

**With cash basis:** Each quarter, you report what actually came in and went out. Straightforward — your bank statements and receipts are your records.

**With accruals:** Each quarter, you report income when invoiced and expenses when incurred, whether or not cash has moved. This means tracking outstanding invoices and unpaid bills at the end of each quarter — more admin, more potential for errors.

For most sole traders, cash basis makes MTD easier. That's by design.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Switching method needs HMRC notification">
If you want to change from cash basis to accruals (or back), you can — but you must inform HMRC and make adjustments to avoid double-counting income or expenses across the transition. Get advice from an accountant if you're considering a switch mid-business.
</InfoCallout>

---

## Quick decision guide

**Use cash basis if:**
- your turnover is under £300,000
- you don't hold significant stock
- you don't have complex work-in-progress
- you want simpler MTD quarterly reporting
- you manage your own accounts without an accountant

**Consider accruals if:**
- your turnover is over £300,000 (required)
- you hold stock or have significant WIP
- your accountant specifically recommends it
- you need more precise period-by-period profitability reporting
