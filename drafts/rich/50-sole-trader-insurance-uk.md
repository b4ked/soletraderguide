## The honest starting point

Most sole traders are underinsured. Some are uninsured for risks they think about daily. A few hold insurance they do not need.

This guide cuts through the noise: which cover matters, what is legally required, and how to think about what your specific work actually needs.

---

## The main types of insurance for sole traders

### 1. Professional indemnity (PI) insurance

**What it covers:** Claims that your professional advice, service, or work caused a client to suffer financial loss.

**Example:** You are a freelance accountant who makes an error in a client's tax return. They are fined by HMRC. They claim the loss against you. Professional indemnity covers your legal costs and any resulting payout.

**Who needs it:**
- Consultants of any kind
- Accountants and bookkeepers
- IT contractors and developers
- Architects and engineers
- Designers and creatives
- Legal advisers and HR consultants
- Anyone whose professional opinion or output could cause financial damage if wrong

**Is it legally required?** Not generally, but regulated professions such as solicitors and financial advisers must hold it. Beyond regulation, many client contracts require it.

**Typical cost:** £100–£600/year for basic cover, depending on your trade, turnover, and level of cover.

---

### 2. Public liability insurance

**What it covers:** Claims that you caused injury to a third party (a client, member of the public) or damage to their property.

**Example:** You are a freelance photographer at a client's office. You knock over expensive equipment. Or a client visits your home studio and trips on a cable. Public liability covers the claim.

**Who needs it:**
- Anyone who visits client premises
- Anyone whose clients visit their premises (including home)
- Anyone working on-site (building sites, events, client offices)
- Tradespeople of any kind

**Is it legally required?** No — but contracts and venues frequently require it. Many clients will not engage you without a valid certificate.

**Typical cost:** £50–£200/year for basic cover.

<InfoCallout type="info" title="Public liability is often required by contracts even when not legally mandated">
Many client contracts, especially in construction, events, and professional services, require you to hold public liability insurance before work begins. If you do not have it, you may lose contracts rather than just face a theoretical risk. Check the contracts you are operating under.
</InfoCallout>

---

### 3. Employers' liability insurance

**What it covers:** Claims from employees who are injured or become ill as a result of working for you.

**Who needs it:** Any sole trader who employs anyone — full-time, part-time, temporary, casual, or even an intern.

**Is it legally required?** Yes. The Employers' Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969 requires a minimum of £5 million cover. There is no exception for small businesses.

<InfoCallout type="warning" title="Employers' liability is a legal requirement — fines of up to £2,500 per day for non-compliance">
If you have any employees, you must hold employers' liability insurance. The minimum required cover is £5 million. The HSE can fine you up to £2,500 for every day you operate without it. This is not optional and there is no minimum staff threshold — even one employee creates the legal obligation.
</InfoCallout>

**Typical cost:** £60–£250/year depending on number of staff and nature of work.

---

### 4. Business equipment and contents insurance

**What it covers:** Loss or damage to the equipment and tools you use for work.

**Example:** Your laptop is stolen from a coffee shop. Your camera is damaged on a shoot. Your specialist tools are lost in a vehicle break-in.

**Note on home insurance:** Standard home insurance policies typically do not cover business equipment. If you work from home, check your policy carefully — you may need to add a business equipment extension.

**Typical cost:** £50–£200/year depending on the value of equipment covered.

---

### 5. Business interruption insurance

**What it covers:** Loss of income if you cannot trade due to an insured event (fire, flood, equipment failure, etc.).

**Who needs it most:** Sole traders who depend on a specific physical location or piece of equipment to generate all their income, and who have little financial buffer.

**Reality check:** For most sole traders who work remotely or can quickly move to alternative arrangements, the value is limited. For those with a workshop, studio, or specialist equipment that cannot easily be replaced, it is worth considering.

**Typical cost:** Varies significantly. Often bundled with other covers.

---

### 6. Income protection (personal — not business insurance)

**What it covers:** A proportion of your income if you are unable to work due to illness or injury.

This is personal insurance, not business insurance. It protects you — not your business — from loss of earnings if your health stops you working.

As a sole trader, you are not entitled to statutory sick pay. If you cannot work, you have no income. Income protection bridges that gap.

**Typical cost:** £20–£80/month depending on your age, health, income level, and deferred period.

---

## What is NOT business insurance

Some accountants and tax advisers sell **tax investigation support** or **HMRC enquiry protection** plans. These are often marketed as insurance but are more accurately a service agreement — covering professional fees if HMRC opens an enquiry into your tax return.

These have their uses but are not the same as business insurance. Do not confuse them with public liability or professional indemnity.

| Insurance type | Who needs it | Legally required? | Typical annual cost |
|---------------|-------------|------------------|---------------------|
| Professional indemnity | Consultants, professionals, creatives | No (but often contractually required) | £100–£600 |
| Public liability | Anyone interacting physically with clients or third parties | No (but often contractually required) | £50–£200 |
| Employers' liability | Any sole trader with staff | Yes — minimum £5m cover | £60–£250 |

## A note on IR35

If you work through contracts that are inside IR35, the contracting business's employers' liability insurance may provide some protection while you are on their premises. But IR35 status and insurance are separate considerations.

If you are outside IR35 — operating as a genuine sole trader — you are responsible for your own insurance in full. The client's insurance does not cover you.

## Decision framework: what do you actually need?

Work through this:

1. **Do you have any employees?** → Employers' liability is legally required. Get it immediately.
2. **Do you visit clients or have clients visit you?** → Public liability is worth having. Many contracts will require it.
3. **Could your advice, designs, code, or professional output cause financial loss to a client?** → Professional indemnity is essential.
4. **Do you own specialist equipment that would be expensive to replace?** → Add business equipment cover.
5. **Do you have savings to cover 3–6 months without income if you were ill?** → If not, income protection is worth serious thought.

Most sole traders in professional services need: professional indemnity + public liability as a baseline. The rest depends on your specific situation.
