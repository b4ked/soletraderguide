# **How to Find a Good Accountant as a UK Sole Trader**

Not every sole trader needs an accountant — but many who would benefit from one don't know how to find a good one. This guide explains when an accountant is worth the cost, what qualifications to look for, how to find candidates, and what to ask before hiring.

## **Do you actually need an accountant?**

Before spending money on an accountant, it's worth being clear about what you actually need.

**An accountant is likely worth it if:**
- Your income is growing and your tax position is becoming more complex
- You're approaching the higher rate threshold (£50,270) or the £100,000 Personal Allowance withdrawal zone
- You have multiple income sources (employment, self-employment, property, dividends)
- You're considering incorporating as a limited company
- You've received an HMRC enquiry or compliance check
- You're spending significant time worrying about whether you're doing things right
- The mental cost of managing your own tax exceeds the financial cost of paying someone else

**Software alone may be sufficient if:**
- Your finances are straightforward (one income source, predictable expenses)
- You're comfortable using accounting software and reading your own reports
- You use FreeAgent or similar software that files Self Assessment directly
- Your income is below £50,000 and you're not close to any key tax thresholds

The honest middle ground: most sole traders with income below £40,000 and simple finances can manage their own Self Assessment with good accounting software. As income grows or complexity increases, an accountant adds real value.

## **What qualifications should an accountant have?**

Anyone can legally call themselves an "accountant" in the UK — there's no legal requirement to be qualified. This means the market includes both excellent professionals and people with no formal training.

**Look for membership of a recognised professional body:**

- **ICAEW** (Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales) — ACA or FCA designations
- **ACCA** (Association of Chartered Certified Accountants) — ACCA or FCCA
- **CIMA** (Chartered Institute of Management Accountants) — less common for sole trader work, more relevant to businesses
- **AAT** (Association of Accounting Technicians) — not a chartered accountant but a recognised qualification; suitable for straightforward sole trader work
- **ICAS** (Institute of Chartered Accountants of Scotland) — equivalent to ICAEW north of the border

Members of these bodies are required to hold professional indemnity insurance, follow ethical standards, and undertake continuing professional development. This gives you meaningful protection if something goes wrong.

**Ask any accountant you're considering:** "Are you a member of a regulated professional body?" If they hesitate or can't name one, look elsewhere.

## **Where to find accountants**

**Professional body directories:**
- [ICAEW's Find a Chartered Accountant](https://www.icaew.com) — searchable by location and specialism
- [ACCA's Find an Accountant](https://www.accaglobal.com) — similar directory
- Both directories show only regulated members

**Referrals:**
Other sole traders in your industry are often the best source. Ask in professional networks, trade associations, or online communities for freelancers. A referral from someone in a similar situation gives you real-world evidence of quality.

**Accounting software ecosystems:**
FreeAgent, Xero, and QuickBooks all have accountant directories of firms who specialise in their software. If you already use one of these platforms, finding an accountant familiar with it makes collaboration easier.

**Local vs remote:**
Most accountant-sole trader work can be done remotely. You don't need a local accountant — you need a good one. Remote-first accountants, often found through online referrals and directory searches, frequently specialise in specific professions (freelancers, creatives, contractors) and charge competitive rates.

## **What questions to ask before hiring**

Before committing to any accountant, ask:

1. **What qualifications do you hold and which professional body are you a member of?**
2. **Do you specialise in sole traders or small businesses?** (Generalists are fine; specialists in your sector can be genuinely useful)
3. **What accounting software do you use and are you familiar with [your software]?**
4. **What's included in your fee — Self Assessment filing, quarterly bookkeeping review, phone and email support?**
5. **How do you communicate — email, phone, video calls, client portal?**
6. **What are your response times for queries?**
7. **Can you give me a clear written quote for what's included?**

A good accountant will answer these confidently. Vague answers about fees or scope are a red flag.

## **What does a sole trader accountant typically cost?**

Fees vary significantly based on location, complexity, and what's included.

**Typical annual costs for sole trader accounting support:**
- Simple Self Assessment filing (one source of income, basic expenses): **£200–£500/year**
- Self Assessment + quarterly bookkeeping review: **£500–£900/year**
- Full-service including MTD support, tax planning, unlimited queries: **£1,000–£2,000+/year**

London and South East practices typically charge more than elsewhere. Specialist online accountants (Crunch, SJD, Gorilla Accounting) often compete on price for freelancers and contractors.

**Monthly retainers vs one-off:**
Many accountants charge an annual retainer paid monthly (e.g., £75–£150/month). This bundles ongoing support, bookkeeping review, and year-end filing. Others offer one-off Self Assessment filing at a fixed fee. For ongoing peace of mind, a retainer is usually worth it as income grows.

## **Red flags to avoid**

- **Unregulated and uninsured:** No professional body membership, no professional indemnity insurance
- **Vague about what's included:** Can't give a clear written breakdown of services and fees
- **Doesn't ask about your business:** Jumps to quoting a price without understanding your situation
- **Promises unusually large tax savings:** Any accountant promising to dramatically reduce your tax bill beyond legitimate allowances is a risk — they may be using schemes HMRC challenges
- **Slow to respond before you hire:** If they're slow answering initial questions, expect the same when you're a client

## **Accountant vs accounting software: the right combination**

Many sole traders use both. The common pattern:

- **Accounting software** (FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks) handles day-to-day records, bank feeds, and invoicing
- **Accountant** reviews year-end figures, files Self Assessment, advises on tax planning, and handles any HMRC correspondence

This combination is often better than relying on either alone. The software keeps records accurate and current; the accountant adds expertise at the points where it matters most.

If you use FreeAgent and share access with your accountant, they can see your real-time figures without requiring you to prepare separate reports. This is the most efficient setup for most sole traders.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: Do I need an accountant as a UK sole trader?**
Not necessarily. Sole traders with simple finances and one income source can often manage their own Self Assessment using accounting software like FreeAgent, which files directly to HMRC. An accountant adds the most value when your income is growing, you're approaching key tax thresholds (£50,270, £100,000), you have multiple income sources, or you simply don't have time to manage your own tax affairs confidently.

**Q: How much does an accountant cost for a sole trader in the UK?**
Fees vary widely. A simple annual Self Assessment service (one income source, basic expenses) typically costs £200–£500/year. A full-service arrangement including quarterly bookkeeping review, tax planning advice, and MTD support usually costs £500–£2,000+/year depending on complexity and location. Many accountants offer a fixed monthly retainer of £75–£150/month.

**Q: How do I know if an accountant is qualified?**
Look for membership of a recognised professional body: ICAEW (ACA/FCA), ACCA, CIMA, AAT, or ICAS. Members of these bodies must hold professional indemnity insurance, meet ethical standards, and complete continuing professional development. Anyone can legally call themselves an accountant in the UK, so checking for a recognised qualification is essential.

**Q: Can I use an accountant who doesn't work locally?**
Yes — most sole trader accounting work can be done remotely. You don't need a local accountant. Many excellent specialist firms work entirely online and focus on sole traders, freelancers, or specific industries. Use ICAEW or ACCA directories to find regulated practitioners, or ask for referrals from others in your field.

**Q: Is it better to use accounting software or an accountant?**
Often both. Accounting software (FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks) handles day-to-day records, bank feeds, invoicing, and MTD quarterly submissions. An accountant reviews year-end figures, handles Self Assessment, advises on tax planning, and deals with HMRC correspondence. The combination of good software and a good accountant is more efficient than either alone.
