# **How to Pay Less Tax as a UK Sole Trader: 7 Legal Ways**

Paying less tax doesn't require complex schemes. For most sole traders, the biggest opportunities are in making full use of reliefs, allowances, and timing that HMRC explicitly provides — but that many people overlook or underuse. Here are seven strategies that work for straightforward sole trader situations.

## **1. Claim every allowable expense**

The single largest source of missed tax savings for sole traders is underclaiming expenses. Every pound of allowable business expenses you claim reduces your taxable profit — and your tax bill.

Many sole traders miss:
- **Pre-trading expenses** (costs before the business started — claimable back 7 years)
- **Home office costs** (often undervalued at the flat rate when the actual cost method gives more)
- **Equipment timing** (buying before 5 April vs after means relief this year not next)
- **Professional subscriptions and training**
- **Mileage at the full HMRC rate** (45p/mile for first 10,000 miles)
- **Phone and broadband proportions**

The test: was the expense wholly and exclusively for business purposes? If yes, claim it.

At the basic tax rate, every £1,000 of expenses you claim saves you £200 in income tax plus 6% Class 4 NI — around £260 combined. At the higher rate, that £1,000 saves approximately £460.

## **2. Maximise pension contributions**

Pension contributions are the most powerful tax planning tool available to sole traders. Every pound you contribute directly reduces your taxable income.

- **Basic rate taxpayer:** £80 contributed = £100 in pension (HMRC adds 20% automatically)
- **Higher rate taxpayer:** £60 net cost = £100 in pension (claim the extra 20% through Self Assessment)
- **Income £100,000–£125,140:** Contributions restore your Personal Allowance — effective saving up to 60p per £1

If you're approaching £50,270 (the higher rate threshold) or £100,000, pension contributions are the most efficient way to keep your adjusted net income below those thresholds.

The annual pension allowance is £60,000 or 100% of your net relevant earnings — whichever is lower. You can also carry forward unused allowance from the previous three tax years.

For a higher rate taxpayer contributing £10,000 to a pension, the net cost is £6,000 — HMRC funds £4,000 through tax relief.

## **3. Use the trading allowance — but only if it helps**

The trading allowance lets you earn up to £1,000 from self-employment without paying any tax or NI, and without needing to keep records.

For sole traders with income above £1,000, the trading allowance can still apply if your actual expenses are **lower** than £1,000. In that case, you can substitute the £1,000 allowance instead of claiming actual expenses.

**When it helps:** Your business income is above £1,000 but your actual business costs are minimal (perhaps under £500). Using the allowance gives you a £1,000 deduction rather than your actual lower expenses.

**When it doesn't help:** Your actual expenses exceed £1,000 — always claim actual expenses instead.

## **4. Time your income and expenses**

The tax year runs 6 April to 5 April. Where you have control over when income arrives or when expenses fall, timing can shift tax from one year to another.

**Accelerate expenses into the current year:**
- Buy equipment or software before 5 April rather than after if you need it soon
- Pay professional subscriptions, insurance, or training costs before year end
- Make a pension contribution before 5 April

**Defer income into the next year:**
- If you have flexibility in when you invoice or receive payment, delaying an invoice from late March to early April pushes that income into the next tax year
- This is most useful if you're approaching a threshold (the basic-to-higher rate boundary, or the £100,000 PA withdrawal zone)

**Note:** Income is generally taxed when you're entitled to it, not when you receive it. Deliberate artificial deferral can be challenged. But genuine commercial timing decisions — like when to raise an invoice — are legitimate.

## **5. Avoid the Personal Allowance withdrawal trap**

If your adjusted net income exceeds £100,000, you lose £1 of Personal Allowance for every £2 earned above that. Your full Personal Allowance (£12,570) disappears by £125,140.

This creates an effective marginal tax rate of **60%** on income between £100,000 and £125,140.

The solution is to bring your adjusted net income below £100,000 through either:
- **Pension contributions:** Reducing adjusted net income directly
- **Gift Aid donations:** These also reduce adjusted net income

A sole trader with profit of £108,000 who makes £8,000 in pension contributions brings their adjusted net income to £100,000 — saving roughly £4,800 in tax (the restored Personal Allowance taxed at 40% = £4,800 saved, plus the 40% pension relief on the £8,000 = £3,200, total benefit approximately £8,000 for an £8,000 contribution).

## **6. Claim the marriage allowance (if eligible)**

If you're married or in a civil partnership and your income is below the Personal Allowance (£12,570), you can transfer 10% of your allowance (£1,260) to a spouse or civil partner who pays basic rate tax.

This saves the higher-earning partner up to £252 per year in income tax.

Conditions:
- The transferring partner must have income below £12,570 (or not use their full allowance)
- The receiving partner must pay basic rate tax (income between £12,571 and £50,270)
- Applied through Self Assessment or HMRC's online service

For sole traders with variable income — particularly those in early years or with seasonal businesses — this allowance is often unused.

## **7. Plan payments on account to reduce cash flow pressure**

This is less about saving tax in absolute terms and more about not paying too much too soon.

If your income drops significantly from one year to the next, you can apply to reduce your payments on account. HMRC sets payments on account at 50% of the previous year's tax bill, paid in January and July. If your income this year is lower, you're overpaying until the January balancing payment.

Applying to reduce payments on account (via Self Assessment or your Government Gateway account) keeps more cash in your business through the year. It doesn't reduce the total tax owed — just the timing.

Conversely, if your income is rising sharply, set money aside based on your likely actual liability, not just last year's figure. Underestimating leads to a large January surprise.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: What's the most effective way for a sole trader to reduce their tax bill?**
Pension contributions are typically the most powerful single action — especially for higher rate taxpayers and those with income near £100,000. They reduce taxable income at your marginal rate. Beyond that, claiming all allowable expenses in full (especially home office, mileage, and pre-trading costs) and timing expensive purchases before 5 April are the most accessible strategies.

**Q: Can I reduce my Self Assessment bill with a pension contribution?**
Yes. Pension contributions reduce your adjusted net income, which reduces your tax liability. Higher rate taxpayers claim additional pension tax relief (20%) through their Self Assessment return, directly reducing the January payment. Making a pension contribution in February or March can meaningfully reduce what you owe the following January.

**Q: What is the 60% tax trap and how do I avoid it?**
When your income exceeds £100,000, HMRC withdraws your Personal Allowance at £1 for every £2 earned above that. This creates an effective 60% marginal tax rate on income between £100,000 and £125,140. The most effective way to avoid it is to use pension contributions or Gift Aid donations to bring your adjusted net income below £100,000.

**Q: Is timing income a legitimate tax strategy?**
Yes — genuine commercial timing decisions are legitimate. If you have flexibility over when you invoice or receive payment, moving income from one tax year to the next affects when it's taxed. The key is that timing decisions must reflect commercial reality. Artificial schemes designed purely to defer income without commercial basis are challengeable by HMRC.

**Q: Can I claim the trading allowance if I have expenses over £1,000?**
No. If your actual allowable expenses exceed £1,000, you should always claim your actual expenses rather than the trading allowance. The trading allowance (£1,000) is only worth using when your actual business costs are below £1,000 — it allows you to claim a higher deduction in those cases.
