# **Income Tax Rates for UK Sole Traders 2025-26: What You'll Actually Pay**

Understanding how income tax works as a sole trader is the foundation of managing your finances well. Unlike employees who have tax deducted automatically, you're responsible for calculating and paying your own. This guide explains the rates, the bands, and the things that catch sole traders out — including the often-missed £100,000 threshold trap.

## **2025-26 income tax rates at a glance**

| Band | Taxable profit | Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Personal Allowance | Up to £12,570 | 0% |
| Basic Rate | £12,571 to £50,270 | 20% |
| Higher Rate | £50,271 to £125,140 | 40% |
| Additional Rate | Over £125,140 | 45% |

These rates apply to your **profit** — your income after deducting allowable business expenses. Not your total turnover.

**Note on Scotland:** Scottish taxpayers pay different rates (the Scottish Government sets its own income tax rates). If you live in Scotland, check the Scottish rates at gov.uk — they differ materially from the rates above.

## **The Personal Allowance: how it works**

The Personal Allowance (£12,570 in 2025-26) is the amount you can earn tax-free each year. As a sole trader, it applies to your total income — self-employment profit, employment income, and other sources combined.

**Important:** the Personal Allowance is frozen at £12,570 until 2031. This means as your profits grow with inflation, more of your income falls into taxable bands — a process called fiscal drag.

**If you're employed as well as self-employed:** your employer uses your Personal Allowance against your salary first. If your salary already exceeds £12,570, your freelance profit is taxed from the first pound.

## **Understanding the tax bands**

Most sole traders operate entirely within the basic rate band. Once your profit exceeds £12,570, you pay 20% on everything up to £50,270.

**Worked example — profit of £32,000:**
- £12,570 — tax-free (Personal Allowance)
- £19,430 (£32,000 − £12,570) × 20% = **£3,886 income tax**
- Plus Class 4 NI: £19,430 × 6% = **£1,165.80**
- **Total: ~£5,051**

**Worked example — profit of £60,000:**
- £12,570 — tax-free
- £37,700 (£50,270 − £12,570) × 20% = **£7,540**
- £9,730 (£60,000 − £50,270) × 40% = **£3,892**
- Plus Class 4 NI: £37,700 × 6% + £9,730 × 2% = **£2,457**
- **Total: ~£13,889**

## **The Trading Allowance: a £1,000 tax break many sole traders miss**

The Trading Allowance lets you earn up to £1,000 from self-employment tax-free, without needing to register for Self Assessment or keep records.

If you're already registered for Self Assessment with higher profits, the trading allowance can be used instead of claiming actual business expenses — but only if your expenses are below £1,000. For most sole traders with meaningful expenses, claiming actual expenses will be more beneficial.

**When it's most useful:**
- You have very small side income (online sales, occasional odd jobs)
- Your actual business expenses are under £1,000
- You want to simplify your return for a low-income year

## **The £100,000 trap: the 60% effective tax rate**

This is the most important — and most under-explained — tax issue for higher-earning sole traders.

When your income exceeds **£100,000**, your Personal Allowance starts to be withdrawn: it reduces by £1 for every £2 you earn above £100,000. By the time your income reaches £125,140, your Personal Allowance is gone completely.

This creates an effective marginal tax rate of **60%** on income between £100,000 and £125,140:
- You pay 40% income tax on that income
- Plus your effective rate rises because you lose £1 of Personal Allowance per £2 earned (each £1 withdrawn from allowance creates an additional £0.40 of tax)
- Combined: 40% + 20% on the recovered allowance = 60% effective marginal rate

**If your profit is approaching £100,000**, this is a serious planning issue. Options include:
- Making pension contributions to reduce your taxable profit below £100,000 (pension contributions are deducted from your adjusted net income)
- Reviewing the timing of income across tax years
- Speaking to an accountant about tax-efficient structuring

Pension contributions are particularly powerful here — a £10,000 pension contribution by someone earning £110,000 can save them £6,000 in tax (60% effective rate), while also building their retirement pot.

## **How to estimate your tax bill during the year**

You don't need to wait until January to know what you owe. Good accounting software calculates a running tax estimate as you log income and expenses.

**FreeAgent** displays a tax timeline in the dashboard showing your projected income tax and NI liability throughout the year. **Xero** and **QuickBooks** also show running estimates, though the presentation varies.

The general approach if you don't use software:
1. Total your profit to date (income minus expenses)
2. Deduct your Personal Allowance
3. Apply the relevant tax rates
4. Add Class 4 NI (6% between £12,570–£50,270, 2% above)

Set aside this estimate regularly — don't wait until January.

## **When does Making Tax Digital change things?**

From April 2026, sole traders earning over £50,000 must file quarterly digital updates with HMRC under Making Tax Digital for Income Tax. This doesn't change the tax rates — it changes when HMRC receives information about your income.

One positive effect: quarterly reporting gives you (and HMRC) a more current picture of your tax position throughout the year, reducing the January surprise. Accounting software will handle the submissions automatically.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: How much income tax does a UK sole trader pay?**
It depends on your profit level. You pay 0% on the first £12,570 (Personal Allowance), 20% on profit from £12,571 to £50,270, and 40% on profit above £50,270 up to £125,140. Most sole traders fall entirely within the basic rate band and pay 20% on profit above their Personal Allowance, plus Class 4 National Insurance.

**Q: What is the Personal Allowance for sole traders in 2025-26?**
The Personal Allowance is £12,570 — the same as for employees. It's the amount you can earn tax-free. It applies to your total income, not just self-employment profit. It's frozen at £12,570 until 2031 under current government plans.

**Q: At what profit level do I start paying 40% tax as a sole trader?**
You start paying the 40% higher rate when your profit (or combined income if you're also employed) exceeds £50,270 in 2025-26. Income between £12,570 and £50,270 is taxed at 20%.

**Q: What is the trading allowance and can I use it?**
The Trading Allowance gives you £1,000 of tax-free self-employment income. It's most useful for small amounts of side income — if your freelance profits are below £1,000, you don't need to register for Self Assessment. If you have higher profits, you should claim actual business expenses instead, as they'll almost certainly exceed £1,000.

**Q: What is the £100,000 income tax trap?**
When your total income exceeds £100,000, your Personal Allowance is gradually withdrawn — reduced by £1 for every £2 above £100,000. This creates an effective marginal tax rate of 60% on income between £100,000 and £125,140. Making pension contributions is the most common strategy to reduce adjusted net income below £100,000 and avoid this.
