# **QuickBooks vs FreeAgent: Which Is Better for UK Sole Traders? (2026)**

If you're a UK sole trader shopping for accounting software, you'll almost certainly end up comparing QuickBooks and FreeAgent. They're two of the most popular options, both claim to handle Self Assessment and Making Tax Digital, and both have a loyal following.

This guide cuts through the marketing. We compare both on the things that actually matter for a one-person business in the UK: price, MTD compliance, how well they handle Self Assessment, and whether they're actually easy to use day to day.

## **The quick verdict**

**Choose FreeAgent if:** you want the simplest possible experience and your bank is NatWest, RBS, or Royal Bank of Scotland (you get it free).

**Choose QuickBooks if:** you invoice clients regularly, need project tracking, or plan to grow and take on staff.

## **Pricing**

### **FreeAgent**

FreeAgent costs £19/month (plus VAT) for sole traders. That's £22.80 per month all in. There is no free tier, but there is a 30-day free trial.

The standout deal: if you bank with NatWest, RBS, or Royal Bank of Scotland as a business customer, FreeAgent is completely free as part of your banking package. This makes the pricing comparison almost irrelevant for those customers — it's hard to beat free.

### **QuickBooks**

QuickBooks Simple Start (the sole trader plan) costs £14/month plus VAT — £16.80/month. It's cheaper than FreeAgent on paper, but the introductory pricing often jumps after the first year, so check the renewal rate before committing.

QuickBooks also runs frequent promotions — 50% off for the first 6 months is common. Worth checking before you sign up.

## **MTD for Income Tax — who's ready?**

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax is mandatory from April 2026 for sole traders earning over £50,000, and from April 2027 for those earning over £30,000. Both FreeAgent and QuickBooks are HMRC-recognised software for MTD.

Both handle the core MTD requirement: digital record keeping and quarterly submissions to HMRC. Neither requires you to do anything special to set this up — it's built in.

**FreeAgent** has been building for MTD longer and has a slightly cleaner implementation for sole traders. The quarterly updates workflow is more straightforward.

**QuickBooks** has MTD functionality but the interface is more complex. For a sole trader who just needs to submit quarterly updates, it can feel like overkill.

## **Self Assessment**

This is where FreeAgent has a clear advantage. FreeAgent can produce your Self Assessment tax return figures directly from your bookkeeping data and submit them to HMRC from within the app. For a sole trader, this is genuinely useful — your SA302 figures are calculated automatically.

QuickBooks does not submit Self Assessment directly. You'll need to export your figures and use HMRC's own service or separate software. This is a real limitation for sole traders.

## **Ease of use**

FreeAgent is generally considered easier to use. The interface is designed around the way small business owners think — income in, expenses out, what do I owe HMRC. You don't need an accounting background to navigate it.

QuickBooks is more powerful but more complex. It uses standard accounting terminology (debits, credits, chart of accounts) more prominently. If you've never done bookkeeping before, there's a steeper learning curve.

## **Invoicing**

QuickBooks has stronger invoicing features — recurring invoices, project-based billing, time tracking, and more detailed client management. If invoicing is a significant part of your workflow, QuickBooks has the edge.

FreeAgent's invoicing is clean and functional but simpler. For most sole traders who send straightforward invoices, it's more than enough.

## **Bank feeds**

Both connect to UK bank accounts via Open Banking. The quality of the connection varies by bank — check your specific bank's compatibility before committing to either.

FreeAgent's NatWest/RBS integration is the tightest in the market — if you use those banks, it's essentially seamless.

## **The verdict**

For most UK sole traders — especially those new to accounting software — FreeAgent is the better choice. The Self Assessment integration alone justifies it, and if you're with NatWest or RBS it costs nothing.

QuickBooks makes more sense if you're growing quickly, work on projects, or need to manage staff. It's a more complete business tool, but that complexity comes at a cost in usability.
