# What Happens If You Miss an MTD Quarterly Update Deadline? UK Penalty Rules Explained

Missing an MTD quarterly update deadline is not an immediate financial catastrophe — but it's not something to ignore either. HMRC has introduced a new points-based penalty system for MTD that works differently from the old Self Assessment late filing penalties. This guide explains what happens, how much it costs, and how to avoid further damage.

---

## The MTD quarterly deadlines for 2026/27

For sole traders and landlords who entered MTD in April 2026, the quarterly deadlines are:

| Quarter | Period | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| Q1 | 6 April – 5 July 2026 | **7 August 2026** |
| Q2 | 6 July – 5 October 2026 | 7 November 2026 |
| Q3 | 6 October 2026 – 5 January 2027 | 7 February 2027 |
| Q4 | 6 January – 5 April 2027 | 7 May 2027 |

The Q1 deadline — **7 August 2026** — is the first submission for most people entering MTD this year. If you have missed it or are at risk of missing it, read on.

---

## HMRC's penalty system: how points work

HMRC uses a points-based late submission penalty for MTD ITSA. The system works like penalty points on a driving licence — points accumulate, and you only face a financial penalty when you reach a threshold.

**How points accumulate:**
- Each missed quarterly update = **1 penalty point**
- Points accumulate across all missed submissions

**The penalty threshold:**
For quarterly submissions, the threshold is **4 points**. Once you reach 4 points, you receive a £200 penalty. Every subsequent missed submission above the threshold also triggers a £200 penalty.

**Example:**
- Miss Q1: 1 point, no financial penalty
- Miss Q2: 2 points, no financial penalty
- Miss Q3: 3 points, no financial penalty
- Miss Q4: 4 points — **£200 penalty**
- Miss Q1 next year (still above threshold): **another £200 penalty**

The points system is designed so that an isolated mistake does not immediately cost you money. Persistent non-compliance does.

---

## How points are cleared

Points are not permanent. They reset once you demonstrate a period of compliance:

- You must submit all outstanding submissions that are due
- You must then make 4 consecutive on-time quarterly submissions with no misses
- After completing 4 consecutive on-time submissions, any accumulated points below the threshold are cleared

This means that if you miss one quarter, getting back on track and maintaining compliance for the next four quarters will clear the point from your record.

## Will HMRC be lenient in the first year?

HMRC has stated there will be a "soft landing" approach in the initial rollout of MTD for Income Tax. In practice, this means HMRC is unlikely to issue financial penalties aggressively in the first year and may exercise discretion in borderline cases.

However, "soft landing" does not mean the obligations disappear. Points can still accumulate, and the compliance record matters for future leniency decisions. Missing deadlines without attempting to submit is not a safe approach.

The safest course of action: submit even if your records are not perfect. A quarterly update with approximate figures is better than no submission at all. You can amend figures after the fact if needed — speak to your accountant if you're in this situation.

---

## Reasonable excuse: what qualifies?

HMRC may waive a penalty point if you have a reasonable excuse for missing the deadline. This is assessed on a case-by-case basis.

**Examples of accepted reasonable excuses:**
- Serious illness or bereavement
- Technical failure of HMRC's systems
- Significant and unforeseeable technical issues with your accounting software
- Circumstances beyond your control that made submission genuinely impossible

**Examples that do not qualify:**
- Forgetting the deadline
- Being too busy
- Difficulty using the software
- Not knowing the rules

If you have a reasonable excuse, you should contact HMRC as soon as possible, before any penalty is charged if possible. Your accountant can help with the appeals process.

## Late payment penalties: different from late submission

Late submission penalties (points system) apply when you miss the quarterly update filing deadline. There are also **late payment penalties** for failing to pay tax owed on time — these are a separate system.

For MTD quarterly updates specifically: no tax is due at the quarterly update stage. Quarterly updates are a reporting obligation only. Tax becomes due through the Final Declaration process at year end.

So if you miss a quarterly update deadline, you are at risk of submission penalty points — not a late payment penalty. The two systems run separately.

**Step 1: Submit as soon as possible**

Log in to your accounting software and submit the outstanding quarterly update immediately. If your records are not fully up to date, do your best with available figures — approximate figures are better than nothing. The sooner you submit after the deadline, the less likely HMRC is to escalate.

**Step 2: Check your points total**

Your accounting software or HMRC's Government Gateway account should show your current penalty points total. Knowing where you stand helps you understand the urgency.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: What happens if I miss the first MTD quarterly deadline in August 2026?**
You receive 1 penalty point. No financial penalty applies until you accumulate 4 points. However, you should submit the missing update as soon as possible and not ignore it — points accumulate, and you will need four consecutive on-time submissions to clear any accumulated point.

**Q: What is the penalty for missing an MTD quarterly update?**
HMRC uses a points-based system. Each missed quarterly submission adds 1 point. When you reach 4 points, a £200 penalty is charged. Each further missed submission above the threshold triggers another £200 penalty.

**Q: Can I get a penalty point cancelled?**
Yes, if you have a reasonable excuse — such as serious illness, bereavement, or HMRC system failure. You must contact HMRC and request a review. Forgetting or being busy does not qualify as a reasonable excuse.

**Q: Is there a "soft landing" period for MTD penalties?**
HMRC has indicated a soft-landing approach in the first year of MTD for Income Tax. However, this does not mean obligations disappear — it means HMRC may exercise discretion in borderline cases. Submissions are still expected on time.

**Q: What is the difference between a late filing penalty and a late payment penalty under MTD?**
A late filing penalty applies when you miss a quarterly update submission deadline — tracked via the points system. A late payment penalty applies when you don't pay tax owed on time. These are separate systems. Quarterly updates themselves don't trigger tax payments — tax is calculated and due through the Final Declaration at year end.
