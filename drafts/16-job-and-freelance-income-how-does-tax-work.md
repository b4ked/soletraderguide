# **I Have a Job AND Freelance Income: How Does Tax Work?**

Many people start freelancing while still in employment — a side project that grows, consultancy work in evenings and weekends, or a portfolio career with multiple income streams. If that's you, here's how the UK tax system handles it.

## **The short version**

HMRC taxes all your income together. Your employment income and your self-employment income are both added up, your personal allowance and tax bands apply across the total, and you report your self-employment income through Self Assessment.

## **Do you need to register for Self Assessment?**

Yes, if your self-employment income is more than £1,000 in a tax year (the trading allowance). You must register by 5 October following the end of the tax year.

If your freelance income is below £1,000, you can use the trading allowance and don't need to file a Self Assessment — but you also can't claim any expenses against that income.

## **How income tax works across both**

Your personal allowance (£12,570 in 2025–26) applies to your total income. If your employment already uses it all, your freelance income is taxed from the first pound.

**Example:**

●      Employment salary: £30,000

●      Freelance profit: £10,000

●      Total income: £40,000

●      Personal allowance: £12,570 used against employment income

●      Basic rate tax (20%) applies to all employment income above personal allowance, plus all freelance profit

Your employer deducts tax from your salary via PAYE. Your Self Assessment return then calculates the additional tax owed on your freelance profit.

## **National Insurance — the part people miss**

This is where employed-plus-self-employed gets complicated.

**As an employee:** you pay Class 1 National Insurance on your salary (8% between £12,570 and £50,270).

**As a sole trader:** you also pay Class 4 National Insurance on your freelance profits (9% between £12,570 and £50,270).

Both apply. There is a mechanism to defer or limit Class 4 NI if your Class 1 contributions from employment already cover the annual maximum — worth checking with an accountant if your combined income is high.

## **What counts as freelance profit?**

Your freelance profit is your freelance income minus allowable business expenses. Track every business expense related to your freelance work — they reduce the amount you're taxed on.

## **Keeping records**

You need to keep records of all your freelance income and expenses for at least 5 years after the Self Assessment deadline. This means:

●      Invoices you've issued

●      Payments received (bank statements or accounting software)

●      Receipts for business expenses

A basic spreadsheet works. Accounting software like FreeAgent handles it automatically and calculates your tax liability as you go.

## **Tips for managing both income streams**

●      Open a separate bank account for your freelance work — keeps records cleaner

●      Set aside 20–25% of your freelance income for tax (lower than the pure sole trader rate because your personal allowance is already used)

●      Register for Self Assessment early — don't wait until October

●      Use accounting software from day one — the cost is far less than accountant fees for sorting out a mess

## **When to get an accountant**

If your freelance income is significant, you're near a tax threshold, or your situation is complex (pension contributions, investment income, rental income on top), a one-off accountant consultation is worth the cost. They can identify reliefs you might be missing and make sure your NI isn't being over or underpaid.
