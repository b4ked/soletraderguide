# **Sole Trader Record Keeping: What HMRC Actually Requires**

HMRC expects sole traders to keep adequate business records — but "adequate" is rarely spelled out clearly. This guide covers what records you actually need, how long to keep them, what format is acceptable, and how the rules change under Making Tax Digital.

## **The legal obligation**

As a sole trader completing a Self Assessment return, you're legally required to keep records that support the figures you submit. HMRC can enquire into your return for up to 12 months after the filing deadline — or longer in cases of suspected fraud or careless errors.

If HMRC opens an enquiry and your records don't support your return, you may face additional tax bills and penalties. The obligation isn't just good practice — it's a legal requirement.

## **What records you must keep**

### **Income records**

You must be able to show the income you declared on your return. This means keeping:

- Invoices you raised (or a log of sales if you don't invoice)
- Till receipts or till rolls (for cash businesses)
- Bank statements showing payments received
- Online payment records (PayPal, Stripe, bank transfers)

If you have both a business and personal bank account, business income records should be clearly separable. HMRC expects to be able to trace declared income back to actual transactions.

### **Expense records**

For each expense you claim, you should keep evidence:

- Receipts or invoices for purchases
- Bank or credit card statements showing the payment
- Mileage log (date, journey, business purpose, miles) for vehicle costs
- A record of home office costs if claiming the actual cost method

You don't need to send receipts to HMRC when you file, but you need to be able to produce them if asked. Digital photos of receipts are acceptable.

### **Bank records**

Keep bank statements for all accounts used in your business. If you mix personal and business transactions in one account, HMRC may ask you to separate them. A dedicated business bank account makes this significantly easier.

### **Other records you may need**

- Records of assets bought for the business (especially for capital allowance claims)
- Records of any grants received
- Records of Stock-in-trade (if you sell physical goods)
- If you're VAT-registered: VAT records in addition to income/expense records

## **How long to keep records**

**Standard rule:** Keep records for **5 years and 10 months** from the end of the tax year they relate to. This covers HMRC's standard enquiry window.

**In practice:** Most sole traders keep at least 6 years of records as a general rule, which provides a comfortable buffer.

**If HMRC opens an enquiry:** You must keep records until it's resolved — however long that takes.

**Deliberate errors or fraud:** HMRC can go back 20 years in cases of deliberate non-compliance. Records from your early trading years may be relevant for a long time.

**Practical example:** Records from the 2024-25 tax year (ending 5 April 2025) should be kept until at least January 2031 (5 years 10 months from the filing deadline of January 2026).

## **What format is acceptable**

HMRC accepts records in paper or digital format. There's no requirement to keep paper originals if you have a clear digital copy.

**Digital records are fine if:**
- The digital record is a complete and legible copy
- It includes all the relevant information (supplier name, date, amount, description)
- You can produce it if HMRC asks

**Receipt apps** like Dext (formerly Receipt Bank), Hubdoc, or the receipt capture in accounting software (FreeAgent, Xero, QuickBooks) create digital records that are accepted by HMRC.

**What's not acceptable:**
- A bank statement alone without supporting documentation for unusual or large transactions
- A spreadsheet total without any underlying transaction records
- Records that don't clearly link to the figures on your return

## **MTD changes the format requirement — not the substance**

Making Tax Digital for Income Tax (mandatory from April 2026 for those with income over £50,000) adds a requirement: records must be kept **digitally**, not just adequately.

This means:
- Each business income and expense transaction must be recorded digitally (in accounting software or a spreadsheet)
- You can't keep paper records alone and summarise them quarterly

But MTD doesn't change *what* you need to record — it changes *how* it must be stored. The same income and expense information HMRC has always required must now be held in a digital format.

If you're already using accounting software, you're likely already compliant with the MTD record-keeping format. If you use spreadsheets, these count as digital records provided the transactions are entered individually, not just summarised.

## **Keeping records without a business bank account**

HMRC doesn't require a separate business bank account — but without one, record-keeping is significantly harder.

If you mix business and personal transactions in one account:
- You need to identify and separate business transactions for each period
- HMRC may ask how you distinguished business from personal expenses
- The risk of missing transactions (and under-declaring income or missing expenses) is higher

A basic business account — many cost nothing — is one of the most practical record-keeping tools available.

## **What happens if your records are inadequate**

If HMRC opens an enquiry and finds your records insufficient:
- They may disallow expense claims you can't evidence
- They may raise a "discovery assessment" — an estimate of what you should have declared
- Penalties apply for inadequate record-keeping separately from any tax owed

The penalty for not keeping adequate records is up to £3,000 per tax year, though this is rarely enforced against sole traders who make reasonable efforts. The greater risk is losing expense deductions you're entitled to but can't prove.

## **A practical record-keeping minimum**

For a sole trader with straightforward finances, a minimum record-keeping system might include:

1. **A business bank account** — all business income in, all business expenses out
2. **Monthly bank statement downloads** — saved as PDFs
3. **Receipts for all claimed expenses** — photographed and stored digitally
4. **A mileage log** — updated after every business journey
5. **A record of any assets bought** — item, cost, date, purpose

If you use accounting software with bank feeds, steps 1–2 are largely automated. Expenses and receipt capture can be handled through the app's mobile receipt feature.

## Suggested FAQs

**Q: How long must a sole trader keep business records?**
HMRC requires sole traders to keep records for 5 years and 10 months from the end of the relevant tax year. In practice, most sole traders keep at least 6 years of records. If HMRC opens a formal enquiry, records must be kept until it's resolved. In cases of deliberate error, HMRC can go back 20 years.

**Q: Do I need to keep paper receipts as a sole trader?**
No. HMRC accepts digital copies of receipts — photos taken on your phone are fine. The requirement is that the record is complete and legible (supplier name, date, amount, description). Many accounting apps include receipt capture that creates HMRC-acceptable digital records automatically.

**Q: Does Making Tax Digital change what records I need to keep?**
MTD changes the format — records must be kept digitally from your first MTD period. It doesn't change what you record. The same income and expense information HMRC has always required is still required; it just must now be held in digital form (accounting software or a spreadsheet with individual transactions).

**Q: Do I need a business bank account for HMRC compliance?**
No — HMRC doesn't legally require a separate business bank account. But without one, separating business and personal transactions is much harder, increasing the risk of errors and making any HMRC enquiry significantly more complex. A dedicated business account (many are free for sole traders) is one of the most practical record-keeping decisions you can make.

**Q: What happens if HMRC asks for records I no longer have?**
If you can't produce records to support your return, HMRC may disallow expense claims and raise additional tax assessments. There's also a penalty of up to £3,000 for failure to keep adequate records. If you genuinely can't reconstruct records (for example, for an old year), be upfront with HMRC and provide whatever evidence you can, such as bank statements, credit card records, or supplier invoices.
